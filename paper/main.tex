\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Programmable packet scheduling.
Emphasize that policies are often hierarchical, and that clients demand line rate.

\paragraph{The reconfiguration problem.}
Consider a small-office gateway running \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: gmail)}, strictly prioritizing real-time \texttt{zoom} traffic over \texttt{gmail} traffic.
The operator wants to add a new \texttt{spotify} flow, and is considering two candidates:
(1) \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, mid: spotify, lo: gmail)}, extending the strict-priority list with \texttt{spotify} slotting between \texttt{zoom} and \texttt{gmail} in priority; or
(2) \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: RoundRobin(gmail, spotify))}, keeping \texttt{zoom} on top but having \texttt{gmail} and \texttt{spotify} share the lower tier via round-robin.
In either case, the state of the art~\cite{vPIFO} would stop the world, drop or recirculate buffered packets, recompile, and reinstall.
The costs are dropped or recirculated (and therefore delayed) packets, downtime, and the rebuilding of nodes that did not need to be rebuilt.

The alternative is to reprogram a scheduler without stopping the world.
Revisiting the examples above:

Transitioning to \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, mid: spotify, lo: gmail)} is actually quite easy, and we can achieve the strongest property we could ask for.
Before time \(t_1\), \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: gmail)} is running; at time \(t_1\) the request to move to \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, mid: spotify, lo: gmail)} is received, and we move to it.
The transition at \(t_1\) is \emph{atomic}, meaning that whatever user-observable interaction (push/pop) happened immediately before \(t_1\) happened entirely in the \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: gmail)} regime, and whatever push/pop happened immediately after \(t_1\) happened entirely in the \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, mid: spotify, lo: gmail)} regime.
This transition happens to be both atomic and \emph{immediate}, in that we do not need a period of preparation before we atomically jump into the new policy; the concepts are distinct.
\xxx[AM]{Can we think of a nice small example that is atomic but not immediate?}

Transitioning to \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: RoundRobin(gmail, spotify))} is not as easy.
As before, \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: gmail)} runs before \(t_1\) and the operator's request arrives at \(t_1\), but we cannot move to the requested policy immediately since there are still \texttt{gmail} packets buffered in the scheduler.
We atomically step instead into a \emph{transitionary period} that is \emph{not} the user's desired policy\footnote{it is \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: Strict*(hi: gmail\_pre\_t1, lo: RoundRobin(gmail\_post\_t1, spotify)))}, but understanding why it is precisely that policy is not necessary at present.}.
After \(t_1\) but before \(t_2\), the scheduler still accepts and emits packets under this regime.
At time \(t_2\), which is defined to be the instant that a well-defined guard becomes true, we atomically step into the user-requested policy.

Although the user never specified the semantics of this transitionary period (\(t_1\) to \(t_2\)), the period is itself a \emph{de facto} packet scheduling regime: the transition is unavoidable, it persists for nonzero time, and during it the scheduler must continue to field pushes and pops.
Something is making scheduling decisions during that window, whether we acknowledge it or not.
It is worth recognizing as a scheduling policy in its own right; we call it \texttt{link}.
Some transitions are better than others from a network operator's point of view, and the state of the art has, perhaps inadvertently, settled on a trivial stop-the-world \texttt{link}.
Our contributions are to be clear about what \texttt{link} is and to improve on it.

Concretely, we take on two obligations.
\emph{Obligation 1: give a semantics to} \texttt{link}.
The transitionary period is shaped by hardware-level manipulations on the way to realizing \texttt{p2}, and not by a clean human-readable semantics; one might naturally worry that it will be hard to pin down the semantics of this transitional regime.
We develop a grammar of atomic edits (\cref{sec:grammar}) and a sequencing layer (\cref{sec:sequences}) that carries us from \texttt{p1} through one or more \texttt{link}s to \texttt{p2}.
The grammar is carefully designed, such that \emph{any \texttt{link} produced by that grammar is itself an ordinary scheduling control in the sense of \cref{sec:policy-dsl}}: so it \emph{does} have a human-readable semantics, just as user-written policies \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2} do.
\emph{Obligation 2: improve upon the state of the art.}
We will give the semantics of the stop-the-world \texttt{link} and use it as a baseline.
Several practical goals guide us as we look for better ways to transition from a given \texttt{p1} to a given \texttt{p2}: can we minimize the length of the transition period, and can we avoid dropped/delayed packets?

We will show that it is always possible to make a transition from any \texttt{p1} to any \texttt{p2}, but it is occasionally possible to make a very efficient transition.

\section{Background \& Motivation}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Hierarchical scheduling on hardware}\label{sec:hierarchical}

A \emph{PIFO} (push-in, first-out queue) is a priority queue that is additionally defined to break rank-ties in FIFO order.
A \emph{PIFO tree} generalizes this to a hierarchy: leaves hold PIFOs of packets, while internal nodes hold PIFOs whose entries are references to the nodes' children.
A packet is \texttt{push}ed into some leaf by simultaneously presenting that packet to every node on the path from the root to the leaf; each node enqueues the appropriate entry locally.
The best-ranked packet of the tree is \texttt{pop}ped by popping the root PIFO to obtain a reference to one of the root's children, recursing on that child until arriving at a leaf, and then emitting the packet that the leaf yields.
This shape captures hierarchical scheduling: each level expresses one scheduling decision (strict priority, weighted fair queueing, round-robin, etc.), and policies compose via parent-child relationships.

A PIFO tree lowers to hardware by mapping each tree depth onto a dedicated processing element (PE), with siblings and cousins sharing the PE at their depth.
Pinning a depth level to a single PE has well-studied benefits for computation and storage; Sivaraman (SIGCOMM '16), Mohan et al.\ (OOPSLA '23), Shrivastav (SIGCOMM '19), and vPIFO (Zhang et al., SIGCOMM '24) all build on this layout.
The PE budget is fixed by the silicon, so the running tree's shape is constrained by what the deployed PEs can host.
\cref{sec:runtime-model} makes this data model precise; the rest of \cref{sec:grammar} builds the syntactic surface on top.

\subsection{vPIFO, and the problem it leaves open}\label{sec:vpifo}

vPIFO (Zhang et al., SIGCOMM 2024) is the closest related work, and it explicitly leaves our problem open.
It virtualizes a single physical PIFO into many logical ``PIFO instances,'' with a Scheduling Description Language (SDL) and compiler, so their PIFO Visor can \emph{flexibly establish} hierarchical PIFO trees of arbitrary shape on fixed hardware; its contribution is the reconfigurable substrate.
As published, however, vPIFO has no notion of a difference between old and new policies, no formal semantics for what a policy change means, and no account of in-flight packets during a change.
Their SDL IR is for \emph{rank computation} compiled to P4 or CPU, quite different from our structural/topological grammars (our \texttt{pol} and \(\delta\), \cref{sec:policy-dsl} and \cref{sec:delta-grammar}).

Our two running examples make the gap concrete.
To briefly break into terms introduced by vPIFO: the Operation Generation Table and the PIFO Instance Address Table are the structures that encode the running tree's shape and per-instance storage.
The easy change, to \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, mid: spotify, lo: gmail)}, looks like the kind of edit that vPIFO's substrate could absorb in place: a targeted append to those tables would plausibly register the new traffic and its operations while leaving the running instances untouched.
The harder restructuring, to \texttt{Strict(hi: zoom, lo: RoundRobin(gmail, spotify))}, lies beyond what the vPIFO substrate can absorb in place: it changes the running tree's shape (inserting a new internal node \texttt{RoundRobin} and re-parenting \texttt{gmail} under it), which is not exposed as an in-place edit on either table.
The closest vPIFO comes is a full reinitialization onto the new shape, which is precisely the stop-the-world baseline we want to improve on.
vPIFO names runtime updating as future work, observing that the scheduling of packets during a modification's transitional phase is unresolved.

Stated plainly: we are not competing with vPIFO and do not claim a better PIFO substrate.
We supply the layer \emph{above} a PIFO substrate (which could well be vPIFO): the formal transition between two policies, the small patch that realizes it, and the transitionary semantics.
The two layers compose.

\section{A Grammar of Atomic Policy Differences}\label{sec:grammar}

\cref{sec:runtime-model} lays out the runtime model: a PIFO tree wrapped in per-node scheduling logic that we call a \emph{control}.
\cref{sec:policy-dsl} defines a small policy DSL and a compiler from the DSL into a control, giving us the syntactic handle on the fully compiled hardware-level scheduler that is actually running.
\cref{sec:delta-grammar} fixes a grammar \(\delta\) of structural edits over that DSL, where every production of \(\delta\) is, by construction, atomically realizable in hardware.
\cref{sec:per-production} proves each production is \emph{sound}: for every production of \(\delta\), we say what running it does to the scheduler that is actually running in hardware, show that this matches the policy-level edit we were expecting, and check that the rest of the tree is left alone, with unrelated nodes keeping their state and the live packets staying accounted for.
\cref{sec:interplay} states the interplay of \(\den(\delta)\), \(\sem{\delta}\), and \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) as a formal theorem that packages the per-production work of \cref{sec:per-production} into a single commuting-square statement.
\cref{sec:hw-preservation} argues that this per-production soundness survives the lowering to hardware.
The productions of \(\delta\) need to be arranged in \emph{guarded sequences} in order to realize a full reconfiguration; this is deferred to \cref{sec:sequences}.

\subsection{The runtime model: PIFO trees and controls}\label{sec:runtime-model}

The runtime object we are ultimately editing is not a bare PIFO tree but a PIFO tree wrapped with per-node scheduling logic; we call the wrapped object a \emph{control}.
This subsection lays out both layers, first the tree (\cref{sec:pifo-trees}) then the wrapping (\cref{sec:controls}), so that when \cref{sec:policy-dsl} introduces a source-level policy language, its compilation target is already on the page.

\subsubsection{PIFO trees}\label{sec:pifo-trees}

A \emph{PIFO tree} is a hierarchy of PIFO queues: leaves hold packets, while internal nodes hold child indices that route each \texttt{pop} down to a chosen leaf.

\paragraph{Shape vs.\ contents}

A PIFO tree separates its structural shape (the underlying finite tree of nodes) from the data carried at each node.
A leaf \texttt{Leaf(p)} holds a packet-carrying PIFO \texttt{p}.
An internal node \texttt{Internal(qs, p)} carries a list \texttt{qs} of well-formed PIFO tree children and a PIFO \texttt{p} whose entries are indices into \texttt{qs}.
This separation is key to making the grammar \(\delta\) of \cref{sec:delta-grammar} well-defined: a structural edit is a change to the shape, distinct from the running contents.

\paragraph{The two observable operations}

\texttt{push(q, pkt, pt)} enqueues \texttt{pkt} into tree \texttt{q} along a precomputed path \(\mathtt{pt} = (i_1, r_1) :: \ldots :: (i_n, r_n) :: r_{n+1}\).
The path is richly decorated: it tells the PIFO of each internal node along the path what child index to enqueue and what rank to use for that enqueue.
The trailing element is asymmetric: a bare rank \(r_{n+1}\) with no slot index, since it tells the leaf's PIFO what rank to use when enqueuing the packet itself.
\texttt{pop(q)} returns the most favorably ranked packet in the tree by popping the tree's root to yield a child index, recursing into that child, until finally emitting a packet from the leaf.
These are the \emph{only} observable interactions with a scheduler, which is why our notion of an \emph{atomic} transition is stated in terms of \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop} observability.

\paragraph{Well-formedness}

A PIFO tree \texttt{q} is well-formed (written \(\vdash \mathtt{q}\)) when, at every internal node with index-PIFO \texttt{p} and children \texttt{qs}, the number of occurrences of \texttt{i} in \texttt{p} equals the number of packets held under \texttt{qs[i]}, for every legal \texttt{i}.
This is the invariant that keeps \texttt{pop} from ever getting stuck.
\texttt{push} always preserves \(\vdash \mathtt{q}\), and \texttt{pop} preserves it when \texttt{q} is non-empty (which is precisely the condition under which \texttt{pop} is defined).

We adopt this much from Mohan et al.~\cite{Mohan23}; the rest of the formalism in this paper is ours, and we preserve backward compatibility with theirs.

\subsubsection{Controls}\label{sec:controls}

A PIFO tree \emph{control} \texttt{C} wraps a PIFO tree with per-node scheduling logic.
It is a tree of triples \texttt{(state, pifo, z)}, one per node of the topology.
This distributed shape differs from FA~\cite{Mohan23}, which packages the same information as a single global triple; the two are straightforwardly interconvertible by gluing the per-node pieces, so any apparatus stated against the monolithic shape carries over.

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{state} is a pair \texttt{(node\_state, slot\_state list)}: the discipline's per-node bookkeeping and a list of per-arm entries, one per child.
When the node runs a discipline without per-arm bookkeeping (e.g., \texttt{RR}), the \texttt{slot\_state} list is empty.
\item \texttt{pifo} is a PIFO: at an internal node, an index-PIFO whose entries are indices into the child list; at a leaf, a packet-PIFO.
\item \texttt{z} is the node's \emph{scheduling transaction}.
It examines the local \texttt{state} and the incoming packet and produces a path segment and an updated \texttt{state}.
We write \(\rightharpoonup\) for partial functions; \texttt{z} is partial because not every packet is admitted at every node.
The shape of the path segment differs between internal nodes and leaves: at an internal node, \(\mathtt{z} : \mathtt{state} \times \mathtt{Pkt} \rightharpoonup (\mathtt{idx} \times \mathtt{rank}) \times \mathtt{state}\), picking a child index \texttt{i} and the rank \texttt{r} with which to enqueue \texttt{i} at this node's index-PIFO; at a leaf, \(\mathtt{z} : \mathtt{state} \times \mathtt{Pkt} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{rank} \times \mathtt{state}\), picking the rank \texttt{r} for the packet's own PIFO entry.
When \texttt{z} is undefined for a packet, nothing is enqueued at this node and \texttt{state} is unchanged.
It is important for well-formedness that, when \texttt{z} is defined (resp.\ undefined) for a packet, it is defined (resp.\ undefined) along the entire path from leaf to root.
This global property is not a concern of node-local \texttt{z}s.
\end{itemize}

We address node-local components of a control by a \texttt{path}, a (possibly empty) sequence of child indices read from the root.
The local triple at the node reached by following \texttt{path} from control \texttt{C}'s root is written \texttt{C@path}, with fields \texttt{C@path.state}, \texttt{C@path.pifo}, \texttt{C@path.z}.
We also write \texttt{C@path.node\_state} and \texttt{C@path.slot\_states} for the two components of \texttt{C@path.state}.

\paragraph{Well-formedness of controls}

A control \texttt{C} is \emph{well-formed} (written \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\)) when, at every internal node of \texttt{C}, the \texttt{pifo} has, for each legal child index \texttt{i}, exactly as many occurrences of \texttt{i} as there are packets stored in the leaf pifos of the subtree under the \texttt{i}-th child.
This is the tree-level condition of \cref{sec:pifo-trees} lifted to controls; stating it directly on \texttt{C} lets \texttt{push} and \texttt{pop} preserve it node-locally, without assembling a global PIFO tree to check.

\paragraph{Equivalence under live operation: \(\sim\)}

\texttt{push} and \texttt{pop} change the live \texttt{pifo}s and \texttt{state}s of a control but leave its structural skeleton untouched.
We write \(\mathtt{C} \sim \mathtt{C'}\) for the equivalence relation on well-formed controls that identifies any two controls related by a finite sequence of \texttt{push} / \texttt{pop} operations.

\subsection{A Policy DSL}\label{sec:policy-dsl}

The formalism of \cref{sec:runtime-model} is a runtime model: it describes the object that runs in the scheduler but exposes no source-level surface for an operator to express a desired policy.
To talk about reconfigurations, we need to expose a programming interface for the network operator.
We design a small policy DSL \texttt{pol} using which the operator can specify their desired policy, and a compiler from \texttt{pol} terms to controls.
The transition planner of \cref{sec:sequences} needs both a way to compile a starting control \texttt{C} from an operator's request and a way to compare two user requests to identify how they differ.
\texttt{pol} is the common syntactic surface both rely on.

This is essentially what the vPIFO paper's \emph{Scheduling Description Language} does informally~\cite{vPIFO}.
They do not pin down a grammar for SDL or formalize the compilation, so our DSL can be read as a formal core of their concrete language.
The compilation targets differ: vPIFO compiles straight to a virtualized PIFO substrate, whereas we compile first to a control (the abstraction that this section reasons over) and only then lower to a substrate (\cref{sec:hardware}).
The strategy, though, is the same: give the operator a syntactic surface, then compile.

\paragraph{Policy syntax: \texttt{pol}}

\begin{verbatim}
flow   in  F                       (flow universe)
pol    ::= flow
         | D(pol_1, ..., pol_n)
\end{verbatim}

A \texttt{pol} is either a leaf labeled by a flow or an internal node running discipline \texttt{D} over \texttt{n} child policies.
\texttt{D} ranges over the disciplines (\texttt{Strict}, \texttt{RoundRobin}, \texttt{WFQ}, etc.).
A discipline may need per-arm metadata that shapes how the arm is scheduled.
\texttt{WFQ} takes a positive real weight per arm; \texttt{Strict} takes a priority rank per arm; \texttt{RoundRobin} takes nothing.
The abstract grammar carries no structural mark of any of this; the operator writes it in the surface syntax:

\begin{verbatim}
rank   in  Nat                     (Strict priority ranks)
w      in  R>0                     (WFQ weights)
sPol   ::= flow
         | Strict(rank_1: sPol_1, ..., rank_n: sPol_n)
         | RR(sPol_1, ..., sPol_n)
         | WFQ(w_1: sPol_1, ..., w_n: sPol_n)
\end{verbatim}

Compilation from \texttt{sPol} to \texttt{pol} carries the per-arm metadata into the arm's \texttt{slot\_state} (see \texttt{init\_slot\_D} below), where \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop} can read it but not modify it; the abstract grammar is what \(\delta\) (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) and the theorems of this section reason over.
We read the arity off by counting children, so \texttt{Strict(zoom, gmail)} is the 2-ary instance of the discipline \texttt{Strict}.
Each leaf label denotes a \emph{flow}: a predicate over packets.

Arm order in the surface notation is a presentation choice, not a scheduling-meaningful one: \texttt{Strict(hi: A, lo: B)} and \texttt{Strict(lo: B, hi: A)} describe the same scheduler.
We formalize this as the \emph{reorder-congruence} \(\eqR\) on \texttt{pol}; the compiler is free to pick any representative of an \(\eqR\)-class when laying out or editing a control (our implementation normalizes and commits to one, \cref{sec:hardware}).

A \texttt{pol} is written against a fixed \emph{flow universe} \(F\): a finite set of flow predicates declared by the operator before constructing the \texttt{pol}.
A \texttt{pol} is \emph{valid} over \(F\) when (a) every discipline is applied at the proper arity, (b) every discipline is provided with the per-arm metadata that the discipline requires, and (c) the leaves form a partition of a subset of \(F\): each leaf carries a flow drawn from \(F\), and no two leaves carry the same flow.
Any packet not matching any leaf-used flow is dropped.
Validity is a condition on the source \texttt{pol}, not to be confused with the runtime invariant \(\vdash \mathtt{q}\).

\paragraph{Discipline compilation: \texttt{init\_node\_D} and \texttt{init\_slot\_D}}

Each discipline \texttt{D} comes with a mechanical recipe for compiling a node that runs it: (a) a per-node scheduling transaction, (b) an initial \texttt{node\_state} for the node, and (c) a \texttt{slot\_state} for each child of the node.
We name the two state-seeding projections:

\begin{verbatim}
init_node_D : () -> node_state
init_slot_D : node_state x meta? -> slot_state
meta?  ::= eps  |  priority-rank  |  weight
\end{verbatim}

The \texttt{meta?} argument is the per-arm metadata that discipline \texttt{D} requires: a weight for \texttt{WFQ}, a priority rank for \texttt{Strict}, absent for \texttt{RoundRobin}.
\texttt{init\_node\_D} is called only at \emph{compile time}, once per node, to seed that node's \texttt{node\_state}.
\texttt{init\_slot\_D} is called in two places.
At \emph{compile time}, walking the source pol, it is called once per child arm to seed that child's \texttt{slot\_state}, taking the parent's just-seeded \texttt{node\_state} and the arm's \texttt{meta?} as input.
When a new arm is spliced under an already-running \texttt{D}-parent (\cref{sec:add}), \texttt{init\_slot\_D} is called once with the parent's \emph{current} \texttt{node\_state} and the new arm's \texttt{meta?} to produce the new arm's \texttt{slot\_state}.

Choosing \texttt{init\_slot\_D} is a scheduling decision, not just a structural one, since the choice changes how a freshly spliced arm competes with the established arms.
We broadly make the choice to ``join the current round''.
For example, if going from \texttt{WFQ(A,B)} to \texttt{WFQ(A,B,C)}, we do not want the newly added \texttt{C} to reap a huge benefit for ``having been silent all this while''; we just want it to join the others with neither a penalty nor an advantage.
The choice plays out as follows for each \texttt{D}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\texttt{RoundRobin}.} \texttt{init\_slot\_RoundRobin} returns the empty tuple; RR has no per-arm bookkeeping.
The round-robin cursor lives in \texttt{node\_state} instead, and at \Add{} we splice the new arm into the child list at the cursor's current position, so its first turn lands in the round already in progress.
The cursor itself is left pointing at the same physical arm it was pointing at before the splice.
\item \textbf{\texttt{Strict}.} \texttt{init\_slot\_Strict(\_, p) = p}: the arm's \texttt{slot\_state} is just its priority rank, drawn from a dense total order such as the rationals so that a fresh priority can always be slotted strictly between two existing ones.
\item \textbf{\texttt{WFQ}.} The \texttt{node\_state} at a parent is the virtual time \texttt{vt}, and \texttt{init\_slot\_WFQ(vt, w) = (w, vt)}: a new arm carries its weight \texttt{w} and inherits the parent's current \texttt{vt} as its last-finish tag.
Any standard WFQ tagging that derives the next packet's tag from the slot's last-finish tag and the parent's \texttt{vt} (e.g., the GPS-style \texttt{max(virtual\_clock, vt) + 1/w}) then slots the first packet on this arm into the round that the established arms are currently in.
At compile time the parent's \texttt{vt} is freshly initialized (to zero), so all original arms get \texttt{(w, 0)} and the round is ``the zeroth''; at \Add{} the parent's \texttt{vt} is whatever the clock has advanced to.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Policy Compilation: \texttt{pol} to control}

We write \(\lceil p \rceil\) for the control compiled from \texttt{p}.
The tree shape of \(\lceil p \rceil\) exactly matches that of \texttt{p}, and at each node the local triple \texttt{(state, pifo, z)} of \cref{sec:controls} is filled in as follows: \texttt{state}'s \texttt{node\_state} is seeded by \texttt{init\_node\_D()} and each entry of the \texttt{slot\_state} list is seeded by the corresponding arm's \texttt{init\_slot\_D} call; \texttt{pifo} starts empty; \texttt{z} is \texttt{D}'s scheduling transaction at this node.

\paragraph{Equivalence on \texttt{pol} and its lift to controls: \(\eqR\), \(\simR\)}

The \(\sim\) equivalence on controls (\cref{sec:controls}) captures live-operation freedom.
We now need two more equivalences: one on \texttt{pol}s under sibling permutation (\(\eqR\)), and one on controls that closes \(\sim\) under sibling permutation (\(\simR\)).

We write \(p \eqR p'\) for the smallest congruence on \texttt{pol} such that, at any internal \texttt{D}-node, permuting the child arms gives a congruent pol: \(D(p_a, \ldots, p_z) \eqR D(p_{\sigma(a)}, \ldots, p_{\sigma(z)})\) for any permutation \(\sigma\).
The per-arm metadata that discipline \texttt{D} requires (a \texttt{WFQ} weight, a \texttt{Strict} priority rank) travels with its arm under the permutation; the metadata is what carries the scheduling-meaningful content, so a permutation does not change the scheduler.

Two controls whose child lists at some internal node are permutations of one another (with the parent's \texttt{pifo} and \texttt{z} renumbered accordingly) present different positional layouts but realize the same scheduler.
We write \(\mathtt{C} \simR \mathtt{C'}\) for the equivalence obtained by closing \(\sim\) under such sibling permutations.
Every \(\sim\)-equivalent pair is \(\simR\)-equivalent; the converse fails.

\paragraph{The bridge: \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\)}

We write \(\lfloor C \rfloor\) to mean ``the \texttt{pol} that \texttt{C} realizes''.
\(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) is pinned down by three rules:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Base case (compilation).} \(\lfloor \lceil p \rceil \rfloor \eqR p\).
The compiler is free to pick any sibling order when laying \texttt{p} out as a control (creating \(\lceil p \rceil\)), and then we read off the exact \texttt{pol} that \(\lceil p \rceil\) realizes using \(\lfloor \lceil p \rceil \rfloor\).
This is not exactly equal to \texttt{p}, but we can use the flexibility that \(\eqR\) affords us to relate them.
\item \emph{Closure under pushes and pops.} If \(\mathtt{C} \sim \mathtt{C'}\), then \(\lfloor C \rfloor = \lfloor C' \rfloor\).
Pushes and pops touch only live \texttt{state} and \texttt{pifo} contents; they leave the topology and \texttt{z} of every node verbatim, so the pol-level skeleton that \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) names is untouched.
\item \emph{Closure under \(\delta\).} Each \(\delta\) has two readings, both defined per-production in \cref{sec:per-production}: an operational rewrite on the live control, written \(\sem{\delta} : \mathtt{control} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{control}\), and a closed-form pol-level effect, written \(\den(\delta) : \mathtt{pol} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{pol}\).
\cref{sec:delta-grammar} fixes the grammar; for now, we just assert that every \(\delta\) we will define both readings.
Rule 3 says the two readings agree: \(\lfloor \sem{\delta}(C) \rfloor \eqR \den(\delta)(\lfloor C \rfloor)\).
The \(\eqR\) slack is needed here because the operational rewrite has arm-order freedom, just as the compiler does.
\end{enumerate}

The three rules together let us propagate \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) from any \(\lceil p \rceil\) along any sequence of pushes, pops, and productions of \(\delta\).
This is how we will discharge Obligation 1 of \cref{sec:intro}: telling the operator what \texttt{pol} is running even when no user has explicitly requested the \texttt{pol}.
The formal interplay of the three rules, and the ``commuting square'' picture that goes with it, is stated as a theorem in \cref{sec:interplay}, once \(\delta\) and its two readings have been fixed.

\subsection{A Grammar for Tree Differences}\label{sec:delta-grammar}

We fix a small grammar \(\delta\) of atomic edits (we use ``atomic'' informally here; \cref{sec:per-production} pins it down formally).
Each production of the grammar has two readings, both defined per-production in \cref{sec:per-production}: an operational rewrite \(\sem{\delta} : \mathtt{control} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{control}\), and a closed-form pol-level effect \(\den(\delta) : \mathtt{pol} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{pol}\).
The grammar is shaped by two demands.
It must be \emph{hardware-realizable}: a production is admitted into the grammar iff \(\sem{\delta}\) can be committed atomically by the hardware substrate (\cref{sec:hardware}).
It must also be \emph{pol-explainable}: every production has a \(\den(\delta)\)\footnote{for transaction-only productions (whose effect lives entirely in \texttt{z}), \(\den(\delta)\) is the identity on \texttt{pol}}, which is what lets the planner (\cref{sec:sequences}) infer a \(\delta\) whose \texttt{pol}-level meaning can be checked and echoed back to the user.

\cref{sec:per-production}'s per-production soundness theorem ties the two readings together: \(\lfloor \sem{\delta}(C) \rfloor \eqR \den(\delta)(\lfloor C \rfloor)\).

Each edit names \emph{where} in the tree the change lands and \emph{what} the change is.
Throughout this section, \texttt{p1} is the presently running policy; we write \texttt{p1@path} for the subtree of \texttt{p1} reached by following \texttt{path} down from its root.
One production is irregular in this respect: \ChangeRoot{} gives the path to the surviving subtree rather than to the edit site.
We explain this in \cref{sec:per-production}.

\begin{verbatim}
d ::= Add          (path, arm, meta?)
    | ChangeMeta   (path, meta)
    | Quiesce      (path)
    | Remove       (path)
    | Designate    (path, survivor)
    | Undesignate  (path)
    | ChangeRoot   (path)

path   ::= []  |  i :: path
\end{verbatim}

A \texttt{path} is a (possibly empty) sequence of child indices \texttt{i} read from the root.
\texttt{pol} is the nonterminal of \cref{sec:policy-dsl}; \texttt{arm} and \texttt{survivor} are field names for \texttt{pol}-typed payloads, chosen to read at the call site (the new arm under an \Add, the designated survivor under a \Designate).

Edits that would have to destroy structure still holding packets are expressly not in the grammar.
For example, structural deletion, \Remove, is emitted by our transition planner (\cref{sec:sequences}) only after ensuring that the subtree being removed is empty.
The richer reconfigurations an operator may want (retiring a subtree that has packets buffered in it, replacing a subtree in-place, pruning a tree down to a subtree) are realized as \emph{sequences} of these productions (\cref{sec:sequences}).

Brief notes on each production:

\begin{itemize}
\item \Add(\texttt{path, arm, meta?}) makes \texttt{arm} a new child of \texttt{p1@path}.
\texttt{meta?} carries per-arm bookkeeping for the new arm, if \texttt{p1@path} requires it.
\item \ChangeMeta(\texttt{path, meta}) overwrites the per-arm metadata assigned to \texttt{p1@path}, interpreted per the parent's discipline: a priority rank under Strict, a weight under WFQ.
\item \Quiesce(\texttt{path}) prevents \texttt{p1@path} from receiving any new traffic.
\item \Remove(\texttt{path}) removes \texttt{p1@path}, which must be empty.
\item \Designate(\texttt{path, survivor}) wraps \texttt{p1@path} into \texttt{Strict*(p1@path, survivor)} in place, making \texttt{survivor} the \emph{designated survivor} of \texttt{p1@path}.
The need for the distinguished discipline \texttt{Strict*} is explained in \cref{sec:designate}.
\item \Undesignate(\texttt{path}) collapses the \texttt{Strict*(A, B)} that lives at \texttt{p1@path} into \texttt{B}, with \texttt{B} inheriting the slot and per-arm \texttt{meta?}.
\texttt{A} must be empty.
\item \ChangeRoot(\texttt{path}) promotes \texttt{p1@path} to the new root, discarding the ancestor chain above it.
The ancestor chain must be a unary ``vine''.
The \texttt{path} argument is interpreted in the pre-edit tree; in the post-edit tree the same node sits at \texttt{[]}.
\end{itemize}

When \(\mathtt{p1} \eqR \mathtt{p2}\), the planner emits the empty sequence (\cref{sec:sequences}), and the live control is left untouched.

\subsection{Per-Production Soundness}\label{sec:per-production}

The two demands that we stated at a high level in \cref{sec:delta-grammar} turn into five concrete obligations that we must discharge for every production of the grammar.
The first four flow from \emph{hardware-realizability}: for the substrate to install \(\sem{\delta}\) atomically, the per-production rule must specify what \(\sem{\delta}\) does, produce a target that is a valid control with fully specified state, and leave the user's \texttt{pop} stream undisturbed.
The last flows from \emph{pol-explainability}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Definition.} Specify \(\sem{\delta} : \mathtt{control} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{control}\) together with the preconditions under which it is defined; outside that region \(\delta\) is \emph{incompatible} with \texttt{C} and \(\sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\) is undefined.
\item \emph{Preservation of \(\vdash\).} \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) implies \(\vdash \mathtt{C'}\).
Any packets or index entries that \(\sem{\delta}\) drops or adds must leave the per-node pifo and packet counts in balance.
\item \emph{Preservation of state.} At every node structurally shared between \texttt{C} and \texttt{C'} and outside the production's local edit site, the local \texttt{state} is preserved verbatim.
At the edit site, and at every node of a freshly-spawned subtree, the state is exactly what the production's \texttt{init\_node\_D} / \texttt{init\_slot\_D} (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) invocation prescribes.
\item \emph{Preservation of observation.} Modeling \(\sem{\delta}\) as a function pins down what it means to be ``atomic'': the substrate commits the rewrite specified by \(\sem{\delta}\) \emph{between two consecutive \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop} operations}, so there is no intermediate state for downstream sections to reason about.
We defer to \cref{sec:hardware} that the substrate can slip this change in.
In this section, we must show per production that the commit is \emph{invisible to the user's \texttt{pop} stream}.
Concretely: every in-flight packet in \texttt{C} sits in some \texttt{pifo} that survives verbatim into \texttt{C'}, and no live \texttt{pifo} entry is rewritten, so a \texttt{pop} immediately after \(\delta\) fires returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have returned.
\item \emph{Characterization.} Give \(\den(\delta)\) in closed form and prove \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\delta)(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).
The equation is up to \(\eqR\), not literal \texttt{=}: \(\den\) returns a definite representative of an \(\eqR\)-class, and \(\sem{\delta}\) is free to land on any other representative of the same class.
Transaction-only effects (e.g., \Quiesce's shrinking of \texttt{z}'s domain) sit outside \(\den\); they are fixed by the operational rule, not by this equation.
\end{itemize}

Write \texttt{C} for the pre-edit control and \texttt{C'} for the post-edit control.
The last four obligations assume \(\sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined; outside the region the production's Definition carves out, \(\delta\) is \emph{incompatible} with \texttt{C} and \(\sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\) is undefined.
We do not repeat this boilerplate at every production.

For transaction-only productions (those whose effect lives entirely in some \texttt{z}, like \Quiesce{} and \ChangeMeta), \(\den(\delta)\) is the identity on \texttt{pol} and Characterization reduces to \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor\) by inspection: no node's \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, or child list moves, and \texttt{z} is not visible at \texttt{pol}-level.
We still state \(\den\) explicitly at each production for uniformity.

Several productions (\Add, \Quiesce, \Remove, \Designate) modify \texttt{z} at proper ancestors of the edit site, even though those nodes' \texttt{state} is preserved verbatim.
There is no separate obligation for these \texttt{z} edits: their \texttt{pol}-invisible content is absorbed by Characterization (which is up to \(\eqR\), and \texttt{z} does not appear in \texttt{pol}), and their effect on the in-flight stream is absorbed by Observation (every live \texttt{pifo} entry survives a \texttt{z} edit verbatim).

The denotational rules below read, overwrite, and shrink child lists.
Let us fix some notation.
We write \texttt{ts[i]} for the \texttt{i}-th child and \texttt{ts[t/i]} for \texttt{ts} with its \texttt{i}-th child overwritten by \texttt{t}; this leaves the arity unchanged.
\texttt{ts[-/k]} drops the \texttt{k}-th child; later children shift left.
Indices follow the \texttt{path} convention of \cref{sec:delta-grammar}.

Several productions edit a single arm relative to its parent.
When the per-production rule hinges on this relation, we destruct \(\delta\)'s path as \(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\), with \(\pi\) the parent's path and \texttt{k} the local index by which that parent reaches the target.

As a warm-up, we discharge the five obligations in some detail for the production \Add.
The remaining productions reuse the same obligations and arguments, so for them we present only what differs in substance: the closed-form \(\den\), the operationally interesting bits of the per-node rule, and the points where any of the preservation arguments departs from \Add's.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})\)}{Add}}\label{sec:add}

We rename \cref{sec:delta-grammar}'s positional arguments for clarity: \(\pi : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the parent node under which the new arm goes, and \(\mathtt{arm} : \mathtt{pol}\) is the policy of the new arm; \texttt{meta?} is the discipline-dependent per-arm metadata of \cref{sec:delta-grammar}.

\paragraph{Definition}

We must define \(\sem{\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})} : \mathtt{control} \rightharpoonup \mathtt{control}\) together with the preconditions on the already running control under which \Add{} is defined.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \Add{} inserts a freshly compiled subtree \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\) as the new last child of the subtree \texttt{C@}\(\pi\).
It also extends the scheduling transaction \texttt{z} at each proper ancestor of \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\), so that traffic can reach \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\).

\(\sem{\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when the path \(\pi\) resolves in \texttt{C} to an internal node, \texttt{meta?} matches the slot-initialization schema of the discipline at that node (present iff \texttt{init\_slot\_D} for that discipline requires it), and \texttt{arm}'s leaf labels are disjoint from those of \texttt{C}.
The disjointness precondition rules out \emph{splitting} an already-running flow into two (e.g., splitting \texttt{gmail} into \texttt{gmail\_business} and \texttt{gmail\_personal}): such a split has to relocate already-admitted packets, which \Add{} cannot do.

The transition \(\mathtt{C'} = \sem{\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})}(\mathtt{C})\) is stated per node.
Let \(k = |\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states}|\) be the new arm's slot index.

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{At each proper ancestor of \(\pi\):} \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, and \texttt{pifo} are preserved verbatim.
The local \texttt{z} is extended to admit packets that classify into the new subtree, mapping them to whichever child slot lies on the path down to \(\pi\).
The new entries do not collide with existing ones: the disjointness precondition forces the new leaf labels to lie outside every ancestor \texttt{z}'s domain in \texttt{C}, so the extension only adds to \texttt{z}'s domain.
\item \emph{At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\):} \texttt{node\_state} is unchanged, and \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states}\) equals
\[
\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states} \mathbin{+\!\!+} [\mathtt{init\_slot\_D}(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{node\_state}, \mathtt{meta?})].
\]
In English: we call \texttt{init\_slot\_D} to find the per-arm bookkeeping that is needed for a new arm under \texttt{D}, and we append that bookkeeping in at the end.
Our arm-order freedom (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) lets us simply append the new child.
\texttt{pifo} is unchanged: no pre-existing entry needs renumbering, and the new arm holds no packets yet.
\(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{z}\) extends \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{z}\) to admit packets that classify into the new subtree, mapping them to child index \texttt{k} at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\).
\item \emph{At every node inside \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\):} the local control is \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\)'s, verbatim.
\item \emph{At every other node (outside \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\) and not \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) or one of its proper ancestors):} the local control is preserved verbatim.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Preservation of \(\vdash\)}

We must show that \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) implies \(\vdash \mathtt{C'}\): any pifo entries or packets that \(\sem{\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})}\) introduces must leave the per-node pifo and packet counts in balance.

At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo} = \mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo}\) contains no entry equal to \texttt{k} (no pre-existing entry can name a slot that did not exist in \texttt{C}), and the new arm at slot \texttt{k} holds no packets, so its well-formedness count reads \(0 = 0\).
Every other slot at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) keeps its index, its packets, and its entries in \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo}\) verbatim, so its matched count is inherited.
Every other node's pifo is untouched (the ancestor \texttt{z} extensions touch no pifo at this instant; they only affect the classification of packets that arrive later).
Nothing needs repair.

\paragraph{Preservation of state}

We must show that at every node structurally shared between \texttt{C} and \texttt{C'} and outside the edit site, the local \texttt{state} is preserved verbatim; and that at the edit site, and at every node of the freshly spawned subtree, the \texttt{state} is exactly what \texttt{init\_node\_D} / \texttt{init\_slot\_D} (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) prescribes.

Outside the edit site the local control (and thus its \texttt{state}) is preserved verbatim, including at each proper ancestor of \(\pi\), where only \texttt{z} changes.
Inside \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\), every node's \texttt{node\_state} and \texttt{slot\_states} are what \texttt{init\_node\_D} / \texttt{init\_slot\_D} (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) prescribe, by construction of \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\).
At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), \texttt{node\_state} is unchanged and \texttt{slot\_states} is appended with exactly \(\mathtt{init\_slot\_D}(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{node\_state}, \mathtt{meta?})\), as required at the edit site.

\paragraph{Preservation of observation}

We must show that the commit is invisible to the user's \texttt{pop} stream: every in-flight packet in \texttt{C} sits in some \texttt{pifo} that survives verbatim into \texttt{C'}, and no live \texttt{pifo} entry is rewritten, so a \texttt{pop} immediately after \(\delta\) fires returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have returned.

No in-flight packet straddles \(\delta\)'s firing.
At the instant \(\delta\) fires, every packet sits in some pre-existing node's \texttt{pifo}, and each such packet survives into the same \texttt{pifo} at the same slot index in \texttt{C'}.
The new slot \texttt{k} holds nothing, and no pifo entry is rewritten.
So a \texttt{pop} immediately after \(\delta\) fires returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have.
Observation is preserved.

\paragraph{Characterization}

We must state \(\den(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?}))\) in closed form and prove \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

We define \(\den\) by recursion on \(\pi\):

\begin{verbatim}
den(Add([], arm, meta?)) (D ts) =
    D ( ts ++ [arm] )
den(Add(i :: rest, arm, meta?)) (D ts) =
    D ( ts[ den(Add(rest, arm, meta?))
                (ts[i]) / i ] )
\end{verbatim}

The base case applies once \(\pi = []\): the recursion has reached the node where the new arm goes, and \texttt{arm} is appended as the last child.
The recursive case walks one step deeper into child \texttt{i} and writes the result back in place.
The \texttt{meta?} argument is threaded through the recursion but is not consumed here: it is per-arm bookkeeping consumed by \texttt{init\_slot\_D} at the operational level.

\emph{Proof of characterization.}
We argue that the structural skeleton of \texttt{C'} matches \(\den(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).
The operational rule above leaves every pre-existing arm structurally intact (the \texttt{z} extensions along the ancestor chain are pol-invisible) and adds a new arm at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\).
That new arm is exactly \(\lceil \mathtt{arm} \rceil\).
Descending down the path \(\pi\) in \(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor\) traces the recursion step for step: at each proper ancestor, step into the child named by \(\pi\); at the node \(\pi\) reaches, append \texttt{arm} to the child list.
For \Add, \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor\) and \(\den(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\) are in fact \emph{equal} as pol-trees, not just \(\eqR\)-equivalent: at each proper ancestor of \(\pi\) the child lists agree pointwise, and at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) both child lists are \(\mathtt{ts} \mathbin{+\!\!+} [\mathtt{arm}]\).
We still phrase the characterization mod \(\eqR\) to keep a uniform shape across productions: \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\Add(\pi, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\ChangeMeta(\tau, \mathtt{meta})\)}{ChangeMeta}}\label{sec:changemeta}

\(\tau : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the target arm whose per-arm metadata changes; it is non-empty (see precondition below) and we destruct it as \(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\), with \(\pi\) the path to the parent and \texttt{k} the slot at that parent.
The payload \texttt{meta} is interpreted per the parent's discipline: a priority rank when the parent runs Strict, a weight when it runs WFQ.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \ChangeMeta{} overwrites \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states}[k]\)'s per-arm metadata with the new value provided.

\paragraph{Definition}

\(\sem{\ChangeMeta(\tau, \mathtt{meta})}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when \(\tau\) is non-empty and the parent at \(\pi\) runs Strict or WFQ.
At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), \texttt{node\_state} is unchanged; the per-arm metadata field of \texttt{slot\_states[k]} (the priority rank under Strict, the weight under WFQ) is overwritten with the new value, and every other field of \texttt{slot\_states[k]} is preserved verbatim.
Under WFQ this includes the virtual-finish tag that records how far slot \texttt{k} has run in the current round.
Other \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, and \texttt{z} are unchanged.
Everywhere else, the control is preserved verbatim.

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\): no \texttt{pifo} entry is rewritten, no subtree is touched, no packet is relocated, so well-formedness is inherited everywhere.
Preservation of state: the targeted metadata field is the intended edit; every other field at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) and everywhere else is verbatim.
Preservation of observation: ranks for in-flight \texttt{pifo} entries were determined when they were pushed.
They remain fixed; the new metadata affects only the rank computation for future pushes.
So a \texttt{pop} immediately after this \(\sem{\ChangeMeta(\tau, \mathtt{meta})}\) returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have.

\paragraph{Note: Why the virtual-finish tag is preserved (WFQ)}

When the parent runs WFQ, the virtual-finish tag at slot \texttt{k} is deliberately \emph{preserved} and not \emph{reset}: resetting would either reward or penalize the targeted arm by yanking it out of the current round, whereas \texttt{init\_slot\_WFQ} (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) was designed precisely to let a fresh arm ``join the current round'' without disturbing siblings, and the same principle applies to a weight change on an already-running arm.
Under Strict the slot carries no comparable round-bookkeeping field, so the question does not arise.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\begin{verbatim}
den(ChangeMeta(t, m)) p = p
\end{verbatim}

is defined whenever \(\tau\) resolves in \texttt{p} and the parent at \(\pi\) runs Strict or WFQ.
Every node's \texttt{node\_state}, child list, \texttt{pifo}, and \texttt{slot\_states.discipline} are preserved verbatim: the rewritten field is in \texttt{slot\_state}, which the compilation rule strips.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor = \lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor\).
Hence \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\ChangeMeta(\tau, \mathtt{meta}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\Quiesce(\tau)\)}{Quiesce}}\label{sec:quiesce}

\(\tau : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the target subtree to silence.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \Quiesce{} restricts \texttt{z}s at various parts of the control to ensure that any packet bound for a leaf under \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) is refused admission.

\paragraph{Definition}

The topology, the discipline at every node, and every arm's slot index are unchanged.
\(\sem{\Quiesce(\tau)}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined whenever \(\tau\) resolves to a node in \texttt{C}.
The empty path is permitted: a root-\Quiesce{} silences every leaf in the tree.
There is no precondition on the subtree's contents: \Quiesce{} is the production that stops further admissions, regardless of what is currently held below \texttt{C@}\(\tau\).

Let \(T\) denote the set of leaves of \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) (the leaves we are silencing), and let \(A\) denote the union of their ancestor chains: every internal node within \texttt{C@}\(\tau\)'s subtree, the node \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) itself, and every ancestor of \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) up to and including the root.

Outside \(T \cup A\) the local control is preserved verbatim.
Inside \(T \cup A\), \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, and \texttt{pifo} are preserved verbatim at every node (including any packets a leaf currently holds and any pre-existing entries at an internal node pointing toward the quiesced subtree, which continue to be honored on \texttt{pop}); only \texttt{z} is restricted.
At each leaf \(L\) in \(T\), \(\mathtt{C'@}L.\mathtt{z}\) drops every packet that \(L\) used to admit from its domain.
At each internal node \(n\) in \(A\), \(\mathtt{C'@}n.\mathtt{z}\) drops any packet whose classifier predicate matches a leaf label in \(T\); for surviving inputs the output is \(\mathtt{C@}n.\mathtt{z}\)'s verbatim.

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\) and state: every node's \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, and child subtree are preserved verbatim, so well-formedness counts are inherited everywhere and \texttt{state} is preserved at every node.
The \texttt{z} restrictions across \(T \cup A\) touch no \texttt{pifo} entry and no stored packet.
They only refuse future \texttt{push}es.
Preservation of observation follows from \cref{sec:add}'s argument: every in-flight packet sits in some pre-existing \texttt{pifo} that survives verbatim, so a \texttt{pop} immediately after this \(\sem{\Quiesce(\tau)}\) returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\Quiesce{} is \texttt{pol}-invisible: its entire effect lies in \texttt{z}'s domain, which \texttt{pol} does not record.

\begin{verbatim}
den(Quiesce(t)) p = p
\end{verbatim}

is defined whenever \(\tau\) resolves in \texttt{p}.
Every node's \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, and child list are preserved verbatim.
Only the \texttt{z}s at nodes in \(T \cup A\) are restricted, and \texttt{z} is not visible at \texttt{pol}-level.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor = \den(\Quiesce(\tau))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\paragraph{Note: Why touch every node on every quiesced push path}

Under \cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s parallel \texttt{push}, every node on the path from a packet's destination leaf to the system root mints an index via its local \texttt{z} independently of the others.
If we restricted \texttt{z} only at a strict subset of those nodes (only the root of the tree, or only inside \texttt{C@}\(\tau\)), the rest would happily mint indices and enqueue them, leaving the tree in a malformed state.
\Quiesce{} therefore restricts uniformly, at the leaf (so it refuses to enqueue) and at every ancestor of every quiesced leaf (so no one mints a stray index).
The cost is touching \(|T|\) leaves and the union of their ancestor chains; the gain is a rejection that preserves \(\vdash \mathtt{C'}\).

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\Remove(\tau)\)}{Remove}}\label{sec:remove}

As before, \(\tau : \mathtt{path}\) is the non-empty path to the target arm being removed; we destruct it as \(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\) with \(\pi\) the path to the parent and \texttt{k} the slot at that parent.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \Remove{} unhooks the subtree at slot \texttt{k} of \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) and renumbers higher siblings down by one.
It is defined only when the subtree at \(\tau\) is empty; we explain this restriction below.

\paragraph{Definition}

\(\sem{\Remove(\tau)}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when \(\tau\) is non-empty and resolves to a node in \texttt{C}, the subtree at \(\tau\) is empty, and \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) does not carry the \(\Strictstar\) flag (the planner reaches a \(\Strictstar\) only through \Undesignate, see \cref{sec:undesignate}).

The topology loses the arm at slot \texttt{k} of \texttt{C@}\(\pi\); arms at slots \(0, \ldots, k-1\) keep their indices; arms at slots \(k+1, \ldots\) shift down by one.

Outside \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) and the removed subtree, the local control is preserved verbatim: this includes every proper ancestor of \(\pi\) (whose \texttt{z} is unchanged) and every sibling arm at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) other than slot \texttt{k}, together with the subtrees under them.
At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), \texttt{node\_state} is unchanged and \(\mathtt{slot\_states} = \mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states}[-/k]\): the entry at slot \texttt{k} is dropped, and entries at slots \(> k\) shift down by one to track the renumbered arms.
The precondition (subtree at \(\tau\) is empty) plus \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) forces \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo}\) to contain \emph{no entry equal to \texttt{k}}, so no \texttt{pifo} entry is deleted; whatever bookkeeping is needed to keep the surviving entries pointed at their (now-resident-at-arity-\(n-1\)) children must be done.
If one were literally indexing children by their positions, that would mean decrementing entries \(> k\) by one; implementations have ways around even this, since the substrate model of \S6.1', for instance, keys children by stable per-edge handles, so survivors' pifo entries are unchanged.
Finally, \texttt{z} is restricted on inputs and renumbered on outputs: packets that \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{z}\) would have routed to slot \texttt{k} are no longer in \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{z}\)'s domain, and for surviving inputs an output of \texttt{(i, r)} with \(i > k\) becomes \texttt{(i - 1, r)}.
Slots \(< k\) are untouched on either axis.

A standalone \Remove{} thus restricts classification at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) so that packets bound for the removed subtree are dropped at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) rather than being routed to a non-existent slot.
In the typical planner usage, a preceding \Quiesce{} has already restricted every \texttt{z} on every push path to the soon-to-be-removed leaves (\cref{sec:quiesce}), so no new packet bound for the removed subtree is even being admitted; the input restriction at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) is then redundant but harmless, while the output renumbering at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) is still needed.

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\): at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), the precondition gives \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo}\) no entry equal to \texttt{k}, so no pifo entry is deleted; surviving slots \(i' < k\) of \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi\) inherit their matched counts from slot \(i'\) of \(\mathtt{C@}\pi\), and surviving slots \(i' \geq k\) inherit theirs from slot \(i' + 1\) (same subtree).
Every proper ancestor and every surviving sibling is verbatim, so its well-formedness count is inherited.
Preservation of state: every proper ancestor and surviving sibling preserves its \texttt{state} verbatim.
No \texttt{init}-rule fires, and \texttt{slot\_states} drops slot \texttt{k}'s entry while every other entry is preserved verbatim, with its position shifted to match the new arm order.
Preservation of observation follows from \cref{sec:add}'s argument applied to the surviving structure: every in-flight packet sits in a preserved \texttt{pifo} at the same slot (if \(< k\)) or one slot lower (if \(> k\)), and every surviving \texttt{pifo} entry points to the same child subtree after renumbering, so a \texttt{pop} immediately after this \(\sem{\Remove(\tau)}\) returns exactly what a \texttt{pop} immediately before would have.
The restriction at \(\mathtt{C'@}\pi.\mathtt{z}\) comes into play only for \texttt{push}es that arrive after this \(\sem{\Remove(\tau)}\) fires.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\begin{verbatim}
den(Remove([k])) (D ts)
    = D ( ts[-/k] )
den(Remove(i :: rest)) (D ts)
    = D ( ts[ den(Remove(rest)) (ts[i]) / i ] )
    when rest is non-empty
\end{verbatim}

The base case fires once \(\tau\) has been walked down to the parent of the removed arm: the \texttt{k}-th child is dropped from the arm list.
The recursive case walks one step deeper into child \texttt{i} and writes the result back in place.
\(\den(\Remove(\tau))\) is defined when \(\tau\) is non-empty and resolves in the input pol; the emptiness precondition that \(\sem{\Remove(\tau)}\) imposes is operational, not visible at \texttt{pol} level.

Proof shape is the same as \Add's: outside the removed subtree every node is structurally intact (ancestors verbatim, surviving siblings verbatim), and at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) the child list shrinks by dropping slot \texttt{k}.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\Remove(\tau))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

Deleting an \emph{occupied} child has no canonical local realization.
Consider \texttt{P(Q(A, B), C)}, shown in \cref{fig:remove-orig}.
Here \texttt{Q} refers to \texttt{A} using the index \texttt{1}, and to \texttt{B} using \texttt{2}.
\texttt{P} refers to \texttt{Q} using \texttt{1} and to \texttt{C} using \texttt{2}.
The tree is well-formed.
\texttt{P} appears to be running a round-robin style policy and \texttt{Q} appears to be prioritizing \texttt{B} strictly over \texttt{A}.
Say the user asks us to delete \texttt{B}, dropping its packets.
To restore well-formedness, we need to remove two instances of the index \texttt{2} from \texttt{Q}'s PIFO and two instances of the index \texttt{1} from \texttt{P}'s PIFO.

At \texttt{Q} the edit is unambiguous.
Well-formedness of the original tree forces \texttt{Q}'s PIFO to have exactly two instances of \texttt{2}, and we delete those two.
The trouble is one level up.
\texttt{P}'s PIFO originally had four instances of \texttt{1}, and well-formedness demands that we remove two of them.
But \emph{which} two?
No entry in \texttt{P} carries the meta-information ``I was enqueued when a packet was inserted into \texttt{B}''; an entry \texttt{1} in \texttt{P} only means ``when this index is popped, recursively ask subtree \texttt{Q} to emit the next packet'' (see \cref{sec:hierarchical}).
This absence of meta-information is a feature of PIFO trees, not a defect: an entry is deliberately detached from the packet it was enqueued with, which is what lets each node schedule its own discipline in isolation and lets a subtree be reconfigured without rewriting its ancestors.
We could make deletion unambiguous by tagging each entry at \texttt{push} with the leaf it is destined for, but that would discard the abstraction: every internal node would then have to track the entire downstream structure, and the composability that makes PIFO trees scale would be lost.

Given this, \emph{we can only pick two instances of \texttt{1} arbitrarily}.
Our choice is, in effect, a scheduling decision, as it affects how \texttt{P} intermixes \texttt{C} and \texttt{Q} traffic.
Keeping the two leftmost \texttt{1}s (\cref{fig:remove-leftmost}), six pops drain \texttt{P} and yield \texttt{c1, c2, c3, a1, c4, a2}: \texttt{A} is pushed to the back, both its packets emerging only after \texttt{C}'s run is mostly spent.
Keeping the two rightmost \texttt{1}s instead (\cref{fig:remove-rightmost}), the same six pops yield \texttt{c1, a1, c2, a2, c3, c4}: this is exactly the round-robin schedule \texttt{P} was configured for, alternating \texttt{C} and \texttt{A} until \texttt{A} is exhausted and \texttt{C} drains alone.

Same tree, same surviving packets, two different service orders.
The \emph{choice of which} \texttt{1}s to drop \emph{silently reschedules \texttt{A} against \texttt{C}}, and nothing in \texttt{p1} or the deletion request records which the operator wanted.

We do not wish to make a scheduling decision for the operator, so our only other option is to reconstruct the tree as if \texttt{B} had never been admitted: drain the tree completely, filter the \texttt{B}-packets, and re-push, recomputing every rank and cursor along the way.
This is a stop-the-world rebuild.

Our grammar sidesteps the question entirely.
By draining \texttt{B} to empty before removing it (\cref{sec:sequences}), every pop that served a packet from \texttt{B} has already removed the matching index-entries from \texttt{B}'s ancestors in the ordinary course of \texttt{pop}: a packet leaving \texttt{B} consumes a \texttt{2} at \texttt{Q} and a \texttt{1} at \texttt{P}, bringing each ancestor's count into accord with its post-drain contents.
At the instant \Remove{} fires, \texttt{Q}'s \texttt{2}-count is zero (\texttt{B} is empty) and \texttt{P}'s \texttt{1}-count is exactly the number of packets still under \texttt{Q} (here \texttt{2}, all attributable to \texttt{A}).
There is nothing left to reconcile and no hidden policy choice to make: the structural deletion is unambiguous.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\Designate(\tau, \mathtt{survivor})\)}{Designate}}\label{sec:designate}

\(\tau : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the target subtree being wrapped, and \(\mathtt{survivor} : \mathtt{pol}\) is the designated-survivor policy that becomes the wrap's low-priority arm.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \Designate{} replaces \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) in place with \(\Strictstar(\mathtt{C@}\tau, \lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil)\).

\(\Strictstar\) is the same discipline as \(\Strict\), distinguished only by a one-bit \texttt{designated} flag that each node carries: \Designate{} sets the bit, \Undesignate (\cref{sec:undesignate}) clears it.
Semantically the two are identical; every push, pop, and well-formedness check treats \(\Strictstar(A, B)\) exactly as \(\Strict(A, B)\), so the obligations below are stated against the ordinary \(\Strict\) semantics of \cref{sec:policy-dsl}.
The star exists only so that \Undesignate's precondition (``\(\tau\) lands on a \(\Strictstar\)'') and a hardware-level argument in \cref{sec:hardware} (that \(\Strictstar\) adds no PE depth) can be stated structurally.
The \cref{sec:policy-dsl} DSL in which the operator writes policies can parse only \(\Strict\), never \(\Strictstar\), so a \(\Strictstar\) is unreachable in any user-written \texttt{pol} and arises only in the middle of a planner sequence, between a \Designate{} and its eventual \Undesignate.
The wrap is therefore \texttt{pol}-visible only as a \(\Strict\).

\paragraph{Definition}

\(\sem{\Designate(\tau, \mathtt{survivor})}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when \(\tau\) resolves to a node in \texttt{C} and \texttt{survivor}'s leaf labels are disjoint from those of \texttt{C} outside the subtree \texttt{C@}\(\tau\).
Overlap with \texttt{C@}\(\tau\)'s own leaves is explicitly permitted; we explain why in a separate Note below.
Let \(N\) denote the freshly introduced \(\Strictstar\) node, and let \(P_0\) denote the number of packets currently held under \texttt{C@}\(\tau\).

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Outside the subtree at \(\tau\), outside \(N\), and off the ancestor chain to \(\tau\):} preserved verbatim.
If \(\tau = \pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\) is non-empty: at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) the \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, and \texttt{pifo} are unchanged; the slot \texttt{k} that used to point to \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) now points to \(N\), inheriting \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{slot\_states}[k]\) (the per-arm meta is held by \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), not by \(N\), so the wrap is transparent to the parent); \texttt{z} is extended as described in the next bullet.
\item \emph{At each proper ancestor of \(\tau\) (including the root, and including \(\pi\)):} \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, and \texttt{pifo} preserved verbatim.
The local \texttt{z} is extended to admit packets that classify into the new arm-1 subtree under \(N\), mapping them to whichever child slot at this ancestor lies on the path down to \(N\).
Pre-existing mappings are untouched.
This is the exact analog of \Add's ancestor \texttt{z}-extensions (\cref{sec:add}).
\item \emph{At \(N\):} for any priority ranks \(\mathtt{hi} < \mathtt{lo}\), set
\(\mathtt{node\_state} = \mathtt{init\_node\_Strict}()\) and
\begin{align*}
\mathtt{slot\_states} = [
& \mathtt{init\_slot\_Strict}(\mathtt{node\_state}, \mathtt{hi}),\\
& \mathtt{init\_slot\_Strict}(\mathtt{node\_state}, \mathtt{lo})].
\end{align*}
Arm 0 is the old \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) (verbatim, including its entire subtree and contents); arm 1 is \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\); \texttt{pifo} is seeded with \(P_0\) entries with slot value \texttt{0} and rank \texttt{hi} (one per packet still held under arm 0).
\texttt{z} routes packets by leaf label: a packet whose label exists in \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\) goes to slot \texttt{1}, otherwise (label exists only in the old subtree) it goes to slot \texttt{0}.
This rule is total on \(N\)'s admitted labels and resolves the overlap case toward the survivor: a label shared between the old subtree and \texttt{survivor} belongs to the survivor from \(\delta\)'s firing onward, while the residual packets under arm 0 drain on their existing entries.
\item \emph{Inside \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\):} the local control is \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\)'s, verbatim.
\end{itemize}

If \(\tau\) is empty, the entire control becomes \(N\), with the old root sitting as arm 0.

\paragraph{Note: Overlap between the old subtree and \texttt{survivor}}

Overlap of leaf labels between \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) and \texttt{survivor} is explicitly permitted.
The moment \(\delta\) fires becomes the boundary between old traffic (which continues to drain from arm 0, \texttt{C@}\(\tau\)) and new traffic (which arm 1 admits): from this instant on, an arriving packet whose label appears in \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\) is routed by \(N.\mathtt{z}\) to slot 1, while the residual packets already under arm 0 drain on their existing \texttt{pifo} entries.
The two arms thus stay operationally disjoint even when their label sets overlap, which is the very property that lets \Designate{} express in-place subtree replacement (\cref{sec:sequences}).

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\) holds at \(N\): by construction the \texttt{0}-count in \(N.\mathtt{pifo}\) equals the packet count under arm 0, and arm 1 holds no packets and no \texttt{1}-entries in \(N.\mathtt{pifo}\).
Every node inside arm 0 is preserved verbatim, so its well-formedness count is inherited.
At \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) (if \(\tau\) non-empty), \texttt{pifo} is unchanged, and the slot-\texttt{k} count \(P_0 + 0\) matches the count that \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) had under \texttt{C@}\(\pi\).
The ancestor \texttt{z}-extensions touch no \texttt{pifo} and route no in-flight packet, so they affect only \texttt{push}es that arrive after this \(\sem{\Designate(\tau, \mathtt{survivor})}\).
Preservation of state is the standard split: arm 0 is verbatim; arm 1 is \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\) by construction; \(N\)'s \texttt{node\_state} and \texttt{slot\_states} come from the listed \texttt{init} calls; everything else is verbatim.
Preservation of observation: every in-flight packet under arm 0 sits at exactly the same position it did in \texttt{C@}\(\tau\), since arm 0 is \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) verbatim.
If \(P_0 > 0\), the smallest entry in \(N.\mathtt{pifo}\) is \texttt{0}, so any pop that descends to \(N\) continues into arm 0 and returns exactly the packet a pop before \(\delta\) fired would have.
If \(P_0 = 0\), then by \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) no pop ever descends to \(N\) (the subtree at \(\tau\) was already empty), so the question is vacuous.
Arm 1 first sees traffic only after a future \texttt{push}.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\begin{verbatim}
den(Designate([], survivor)) p =
    Strict(p, survivor)
den(Designate(i :: r, survivor)) (D ts) =
    D ( ts[ den(Designate(r, survivor))
                (ts[i]) / i ] )
\end{verbatim}

\(\den\) returns \(\Strict\), not \(\Strictstar\): the designated bit is \texttt{pol}-invisible.
The proof is the same shape as \Add's: the operational rule leaves every node outside the wrap structurally intact, the new arm 1 is \(\lceil \mathtt{survivor} \rceil\), and the new \(\Strictstar\) reads as \(\Strict\) at \texttt{pol}-level.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\Designate(\tau, \mathtt{survivor}))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\Undesignate(\tau)\)}{Undesignate}}\label{sec:undesignate}

\(\tau : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the target \(\Strictstar\) node being unwrapped.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \Undesignate{} unwraps the \(\Strictstar(A, B)\) at \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) whose \(A\)-arm is empty, leaving \(B\) in its slot.
It is the structural inverse of \Designate.

\paragraph{Definition}

\(\sem{\Undesignate(\tau)}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when \(\tau\) resolves to \texttt{C@}\(\tau\), \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) carries the \texttt{designated} bit (so it is a \(\Strictstar\) introduced by an earlier \Designate), and arm 0 of \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) is empty.
Let \(N = \mathtt{C@}\tau\).

Inside \(B\) (arm 1 of \(N\)), every node is preserved verbatim.
If \(\tau = \pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\) is non-empty, then at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) the \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{pifo}, and \texttt{z} are unchanged; \texttt{slot\_states[k]} is preserved verbatim (the wrapper inherited it at \Designate{} time, and the unwrap returns it unchanged); slot \texttt{k} now points to \(B\) rather than to \(N\); and \(N\) itself and its arm-0 stub are discarded.
If \(\tau\) is empty, the new root is \(B\), inheriting \(B\)'s own \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, \texttt{z}, and child list verbatim, and \(N\) and its arm-0 stub are discarded.

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\): at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\), \texttt{pifo} is unchanged; slot \texttt{k}'s count was \(0 + |B|\) under \(N\) and is now \(|B|\) under \(B\) directly, so the count matches.
Inside \(B\), every node is verbatim.
The discarded \(N.\mathtt{pifo}\) was carrying its own count of arm-1 entries one level too deep: by \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) and the empty-arm-0 precondition, that count equals \(|B|\), which is exactly what \(\mathtt{C@}\pi.\mathtt{pifo}\)'s \texttt{k}-entries already carry one level up.
After the unwrap, those \texttt{k}-entries route directly to \(B\), whose pifo count is still \(|B|\), so the well-formedness obligation at \texttt{C@}\(\pi\) reads \(|B| = |B|\) and inside \(B\) reads as before.
The previously-redundant routing step (``\(\Strictstar\) says go to arm 1,'' which arm 0 being empty had already forced) is the only step elided.
Preservation of state: standard verbatim everywhere outside \(N\); \(N\)'s local state vanishes.
Preservation of observation: arm 0 holds no packet by precondition, so \(\Strictstar\)'s ``favor arm 0'' behavior was already routing every pop to arm 1, and the unwrap preserves this routing exactly.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\begin{verbatim}
den(Undesignate([])) (Strict(A, B))
    = B
den(Undesignate(i :: r)) (D ts)
    = D ( ts[ den(Undesignate(r)) (ts[i]) / i ] )
\end{verbatim}

The base case is defined when the input matches \(\Strict(\_, \_)\); the operational rule additionally requires that arm 0 of \texttt{C@}\(\tau\) be empty at the moment \(\delta\) fires.
The base case consumes the \(\Strict\) wrapper (which on the \texttt{pol} side is what the \(\Strictstar\) reads as).
Proof shape is \Remove's: outside \(N\) nothing structural moves, and at \(N\) the \(\Strict\) wrapper is dropped.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\Undesignate(\tau))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{\(\ChangeRoot(\nu)\)}{ChangeRoot}}\label{sec:changeroot}

\(\nu : \mathtt{path}\) is the path to the subtree being promoted to the new root.

Say the presently running control is \texttt{C}.
Intuitively, \ChangeRoot{} promotes \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) to the new root, discarding every ancestor above it.
It is the only production that erases structural ancestors, and the only one whose \texttt{pol}-level effect is to shrink the tree from above rather than edit it locally.
The mechanics are short; the notes below are substantive.

\paragraph{Definition}

\(\sem{\ChangeRoot(\nu)}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined when \(\nu\) is non-empty, resolves to a node in \texttt{C}, and every internal node strictly above \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) is \emph{unary}, with its sole arm continuing toward \texttt{C@}\(\nu\).
Under these preconditions the discarded ancestor chain carries scheduling metadata but no off-path traffic.

At \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) and inside its subtree, everything is preserved verbatim: the result \(\mathtt{C'}\) is exactly \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) standing alone as a tree.
At every proper ancestor of \(\nu\), the local \texttt{node\_state}, \texttt{slot\_states}, \texttt{pifo}, and \texttt{z} are discarded entirely.

\paragraph{Preservation}

Preservation of \(\vdash\): \(\vdash \mathtt{C}\) is a conjunction of per-node well-formedness obligations, and the subset of those obligations attached to \texttt{C@}\(\nu\)'s nodes constitutes \(\vdash \mathtt{C@}\nu\), which is exactly \(\vdash \mathtt{C'}\).
The discarded ancestors' obligations are simply forgotten.
Preservation of state: standard verbatim within \texttt{C@}\(\nu\); ancestor state is gone.
Preservation of observation: every in-flight packet sits in some \texttt{pifo} within \texttt{C@}\(\nu\)'s subtree (the ancestor \texttt{pifo}s held routing entries, not packets), all preserved verbatim.
A pop immediately after this \(\sem{\ChangeRoot(\nu)}\) returns exactly what a pop immediately before would have: a pop before \(\delta\) fires descends through the unary chain by popping each ancestor's PIFO in turn, and since each such PIFO has a single slot (slot \texttt{0}), the popped entry is forced to be a slot-\texttt{0} entry regardless of its rank; the descent therefore lands at \texttt{C@}\(\nu\)'s root and pops from there.
A pop after \(\delta\) fires acts on \texttt{C@}\(\nu\)'s root directly, returning the same packet.

\paragraph{Characterization}

\begin{verbatim}
den(ChangeRoot(v)) p = p@v
\end{verbatim}

is defined whenever \(\nu\) is non-empty and resolves in \texttt{p}.
The realizes-relation propagates structurally: if \texttt{C} realizes \texttt{p}, then \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) realizes \(\mathtt{p@}\nu\), since compilation is per-node and the discipline at each surviving node is unchanged.
So \(\lfloor \mathtt{C'} \rfloor \eqR \mathtt{p@}\nu = \den(\ChangeRoot(\nu))(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).

\paragraph{Notes}

\emph{Why the unary precondition.}
The precondition rules out, by construction, any silently-dropped packet-bearing siblings.
A non-unary ancestor would have arms branching off the path to \texttt{C@}\(\nu\); those subtrees would vanish with their ancestor, taking their packets with them.
Allowing this would make \ChangeRoot{} a structural deletion of arbitrarily many off-path subtrees masquerading as a root change, with no semantic story for those packets.
The richer reconfiguration of pruning to a subtree whose ancestor chain branches off into packet-bearing relatives is realized as the \PruneDownTo{} idiom (\cref{sec:idioms}), which first drains and \Remove{}s those siblings in sequence, reducing the chain above \texttt{C@}\(\nu\) to the unary shape that \ChangeRoot{} then accepts.

\emph{What the chain carries, and what is discarded.}
An internal node's \texttt{node\_state}, rank function, and \texttt{pifo} ordering exist to pick among siblings.
At a unary node the sibling-picking role degenerates: with one arm, every pop is forced into it.

What can remain active at a unary node is rank computation that depends on per-packet attributes rather than on sibling identity.
If a chain's disciplines do no such per-packet reordering, each unary node along the chain is operationally a passthrough, and the immediate-pop observation-preservation above extends to every subsequent pop and push.
If they do (LSTF~\cite{Mittal16} is a canonical example, with rank determined by each packet's deadline), the chain is actively shaping traffic, and \ChangeRoot{} removes that shaping.
That is precisely the production's intended role: the atomic step by which the operator says ``promote \(\mathtt{p1@}\nu\) to the root and discard the ancestor shaping above it.''
The \PruneDownTo{} idiom packages this with the upstream draining and \Remove{}s that make the chain unary in the first place, so the operator's request (``prune to this subtree'') is realized as a sequence whose final step discards exactly the ancestor influence that the operator has chosen to abandon.

\emph{Pol-level effect.}
The \texttt{pol} changes from the unary chain wrapping \(\mathtt{p1@}\nu\) (e.g., \(\Strict(\mathtt{p1@}\nu)\) or \(\mathtt{LSTF}(\mathtt{p1@}\nu)\)) to just \(\mathtt{p1@}\nu\).
This is \texttt{pol}-visible: the root discipline observably changes, which is the substantive content of the production.
Whether that pol-visible change reorders in-flight traffic depends on whether the discarded chain was doing per-packet reordering (see previous Note).

\subsection{The Interplay of \(\den(\delta)\) and \(\sem{\delta}\)}\label{sec:interplay}

\xxx[AM]{I could literally make this a new Section 4 if you'd rather promote it (that is what Adrian literally asked for), but I've provisionally kept it as a subsection to save on renumbering roughly forty downstream \cref{sec:sequences}/\cref{sec:planner}/\cref{sec:hardware} references.}

\crefrange{sec:runtime-model}{sec:per-production} introduced all the moving parts: the runtime object \texttt{C}, the source \texttt{pol}, the compilation \(\lceil \cdot \rceil\), the reading-off \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\), the atomic-diff grammar \(\delta\), and its two readings \(\den(\delta)\) and \(\sem{\delta}\).
This subsection states formally how they fit together.
The interplay is what lets the runtime, at every step, echo to the operator the \texttt{pol} that the live control realizes.

The situation we care about is a single \(\delta\) firing on a running control.
Picture a commuting square:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
  node distance=1.2cm and 3.2cm,
  every node/.style={font=\small},
  ->, >={Stealth[length=4pt]}
]
\node (p1) {\texttt{p1}};
\node (p2) [right=of p1] {\texttt{p2}};

\node[inner xsep=1pt, right=1pt of c1] (c1p) {$\sim$ \texttt{C1'}};
\node[inner xsep=1pt] (c2) [below=of p2] {\texttt{C2}};
\node[inner xsep=1pt, left=1pt of c2] (c2p) {\texttt{C2'} $\simR$};
\draw (p1) -- node[above]{$\den(\delta)$} (p2);
\draw (c1p) -- node[above]{$\sem{\delta}$} (c2p);
\draw (p1) -- node[left]{$\lfloor\cdot\rfloor$} (c1);
\draw (p2) -- node[right]{$\lfloor\cdot\rfloor$} (c2);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Read it with an eye to the user's experience.
Our final goal will be to correctly relate \texttt{C2'} and \texttt{C2}.

The operator writes \texttt{p1}, so \(\mathtt{C1} := \lceil \mathtt{p1} \rceil\), with \(\lfloor \mathtt{C1} \rfloor \eqR \mathtt{p1}\) by rule 1 of \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}).
We echo \(\mathtt{p1'} := \lfloor \mathtt{C1} \rfloor\) back to the user; this is not shown in the diagram but will become important shortly.
\texttt{p1'} faithfully represents the arm ordering that the compiler chose.
Push and pop operations then carry \texttt{C1} to \texttt{C1'}: \(\mathtt{C1} \sim \mathtt{C1'}\) by the definition of \(\sim\), and \(\lfloor \mathtt{C1'} \rfloor = \lfloor \mathtt{C1} \rfloor\) by rule 2 of \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\), so the live \texttt{C1'} still realizes both \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p1'}.

The operator writes \texttt{p2}, and the \emph{transition planner} (\crefrange{sec:sequences}{sec:planner}), the tool that infers atomic edits between two \texttt{pol}s, produces a \(\delta\) such that \(\den(\delta)(\mathtt{p1'}) \eqR \mathtt{p2}\).
It is key that we work in the frame of the actually-running representative \texttt{p1'} rather than the operator's original \texttt{p1}, since \(\den\) is stated using semantically meaningful paths.
Applying \(\sem{\delta}\) to \texttt{C1'} brings us to control \texttt{C2'}, and by rule 3 of \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) we have \(\lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor \eqR \den(\delta)(\mathtt{p1'})\); chaining this with the previously-established \(\den(\delta)(\mathtt{p1'}) \eqR \mathtt{p2}\) gives \(\lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor \eqR \mathtt{p2}\).
We again echo \(\mathtt{p2'} := \lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor\) to the user.

The transformation is complete at this point, but we need to ground ourselves.
\(\mathtt{C2} := \lceil \mathtt{p2} \rceil\) is the control we \emph{would have built} had we taken the SOTA stop-the-world path: the state-of-the-art response to a \(\mathtt{p1} \to \mathtt{p2}\) request is to freshly compile \(\lceil \mathtt{p2} \rceil\) (\cref{sec:intro}).
We do not actually construct \texttt{C2}, but it is the correct reference point and it is crucial that we now relate \texttt{C2'} (which we have just produced after a fashion) to \texttt{C2}.
By rule 1, \(\lfloor \mathtt{C2} \rfloor \eqR \mathtt{p2}\), hence \(\lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor \eqR \lfloor \mathtt{C2} \rfloor\).
But we would like to relate the controls directly, not just their \texttt{pol}-level projections.
The relation we write is \(\mathtt{C2'} \simR \mathtt{C2}\), which absorbs two gaps at once.
\texttt{C2'} carries the live \texttt{pifo}/\texttt{state} accumulated since \texttt{C1}, while \texttt{C2} is freshly compiled and bare (the \(\sim\) component covers this); and \(\sem{\delta}\) and \(\lceil \cdot \rceil\) are free to pick different sibling orders at internal nodes, so \texttt{C2'} and \texttt{C2} may also differ in child arrangements (the R-closure covers this).

The diagram is intuition; the formal statement is:

\begin{theorem}[Interplay of \(\den(\delta)\) and \(\sem{\delta}\)]\label{thm:interplay}
Let \texttt{C} be a well-formed control, and let \(\delta\) be a production of the grammar (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) that is compatible with \texttt{C} (that is, \(\sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\) is defined).
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(\vdash \sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\).
\item \(\lfloor \sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C}) \rfloor \eqR \den(\delta)(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each clause is an aggregation of per-production obligations already discharged in \cref{sec:per-production}.
Clause 1 is exactly \emph{Preservation of \(\vdash\)}; clause 2 is exactly \emph{Characterization}.
\end{proof}

The diagram above is the unit cell of a fuller picture.
A typical reconfiguration is not a single \(\delta\) but a guarded sequence \((\varphi_0 ; \delta_0) ; \ldots ; (\varphi_n ; \delta_n)\); \cref{sec:idioms} stacks copies of this unit cell horizontally, with each intermediate control \(\mathtt{link}_i\) between \texttt{C} and \texttt{C'} carrying its own pol-level meaning \(\lfloor \mathtt{link}_i \rfloor\).
For this subsection's purposes we read the unit cell on its own; everything that follows about a single \(\delta\), including the operator-advice below, lifts cell-by-cell to the sequence picture in \cref{sec:idioms}.

When writing \texttt{p2}, the operator would do well to state their request against \texttt{p1'}, the actually-running \texttt{pol} that we echoed back to them.
This keeps \(\delta\) small and aligns paths with what is running.
The operator may state \texttt{p2} against the \texttt{p1} they originally wrote, but at their own risk.
On the milder end, the planner may infer a larger \(\delta\) than necessary, or may give up.
More seriously, if the user is in Imperative Mode (\cref{sec:sequences}) and bases their paths on \texttt{p1}, they may inadvertently edit the wrong node or provide a malformed path.

\paragraph{A worked example}

The operator requests \texttt{p1 = Strict(hi: zoom, lo: gmail)}.
The compiler uses its degree of freedom to yield control \texttt{C1} such that \(\lfloor \mathtt{C1} \rfloor = \mathtt{Strict(lo: gmail, hi: zoom)}\); the children have been reordered for some compiler-internal reason.
We echo back
\[
\mathtt{p1'} := \lfloor \mathtt{C1} \rfloor = \mathtt{Strict(lo: gmail, hi: zoom)}.
\]
We know that \(\mathtt{p1} \eqR \mathtt{p1'}\), so the scheduler the operator gets is the one they asked for; only the slot numbering differs.
As the control serves pushes and pops, it transforms into \texttt{C1'}.

Later, the operator requests \texttt{p2 = Strict(lo: gmail, mid: spotify, hi: zoom)}.
The runtime can again use its freedom: instead of literally splicing \texttt{spotify} in between running arms \texttt{gmail} and \texttt{zoom}, it chooses to append \texttt{spotify} to the end.
This converts the running control \texttt{C1'} into control \texttt{C2'} such that
\[
\lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor = \mathtt{Strict(lo: gmail, hi: zoom, mid: spotify)},
\]
and we echo \(\mathtt{p2'} := \lfloor \mathtt{C2'} \rfloor\) back to the user.

Now the operator changes to Imperative Mode (\cref{sec:sequences}) and writes the path-bearing edit \texttt{(True, Quiesce([2]))}.
The syntax and semantics do not matter here; what matters is that the operator has requested an edit and identified the target via path ``\texttt{[2]}''.
Paths are interpreted against the \emph{actually running} representative \texttt{p2'}, so we \Quiesce{} the subtree \texttt{mid: spotify} (\texttt{p2'}'s third slot), not \texttt{lo: gmail} (\texttt{p2}'s third slot).
If the operator had based the path on \texttt{p2} they would have edited the wrong arm; the system has no way to detect or recover from that.

\subsection{Preserving this proof down to hardware}\label{sec:hw-preservation}

\cref{sec:per-production} proves soundness at the \(\delta\) level, where each production (\Add, \Quiesce, \Remove, \ldots) carries a well-formed tree to a well-formed tree.
To run on hardware, each edit is lowered, by a simple and mechanical compilation, into a sequence of fine-grained instructions in our IR.
The IR alphabet and the substrate machinery are the subject of \cref{sec:hardware}; here we only need that the IR has fine-grained verbs (we will name \texttt{spawn}, \texttt{adopt}, \texttt{emancipate}, \texttt{assoc}, \texttt{deassoc}, and the like) and that a single such verb, unlike a whole production of \(\delta\), \emph{can} leave the tree malformed: a node freshly created by \texttt{spawn} has not yet been wired to its parent by \texttt{adopt}, for example.

We do not prove soundness at the IR level, but instead informally make the case for why the \cref{sec:per-production} proof survives the lowering.
There are two reasons.
First, the compilation is \emph{faithful}: each production of \(\delta\) expands to a fixed instruction sequence that, when run to completion, realizes exactly that production's effect.
We give the command-to-commands translation and take its faithfulness to be uncontroversial.
Second, our substrate runs each such sequence as a single \emph{transactional commit}: a bracketed group of IR instructions that installs as one instant from the user's perspective, with no \texttt{push} or \texttt{pop} interleaved between the bracket's open and close (\S6.1' spells out the substrate machinery that makes this so).
The transiently-malformed intermediate trees are therefore never observed.
That commit is precisely how the substrate \emph{realizes} the atomicity property of \cref{sec:grammar}: atomicity asked for an instantaneous control replacement between two user operations, and the commit is what collapses a multi-instruction lowering into one such instant.
Every \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop} therefore still lands on a well-formed control (\texttt{p1}, some \texttt{link}, or \texttt{p2}), exactly as \cref{sec:per-production} proved; the IR's transient malformedness lives entirely inside commits, invisible to the user.

The same argument carries from the IR down to hardware: the hardware executes a committed sequence atomically with respect to user operations, so what it exhibits is again what \cref{sec:per-production} proved.
The compilation itself, and the substrate machinery that makes a commit atomic, are the subject of \cref{sec:hardware}.

\xxx[AM for Zhiyuan]{the \cref{sec:hw-preservation} argument leans on the substrate supporting \emph{atomic transactional commits}: a multi-instruction lowering must install as a single instant from the user's perspective, so that the transiently-malformed intermediate trees inside a commit are never observed.
Our own substrate provides this.
The open question for \cref{sec:hardware} is whether composition with a third-party substrate (e.g., vPIFO) requires the same property and, if so, whether vPIFO offers it.
Flagged here so the \cref{sec:hw-preservation} claim ``the proof survives the lowering'' is not read as substrate-independent.}

\section{Realizing Reconfigurations as Guarded Sequences}\label{sec:sequences}

This section composes the productions of \(\delta\) (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) into \emph{guarded sequences} \((\varphi ; \delta)^{*}\), where a guard \(\varphi\) is a predicate on the state of the live control.
Guarded sequences realize changes to the live control that no single \(\delta\) can express.

A guarded sequence threads the live control through a chain of intermediate controls, one per pair of consecutive productions.
We call each such intermediate control a \texttt{link}, writing \(\mathtt{link}_i\) for the control on which the \texttt{i}-th \(\delta\) (zero-indexed) fires; \(\mathtt{link}_0 = \mathtt{C}\) is the starting control, and \(\mathtt{link}_{i+1} = \sem{\delta_i}(\mathtt{link}_i)\) is what the \texttt{i}-th \(\delta\) leaves behind (\cref{sec:idioms} makes this precise).

The main observation of the section, developed in \cref{sec:idioms}, is that each \texttt{link} is itself an ordinary \cref{sec:runtime-model}-style control, so the ``transitionary period'' \emph{needs no new semantics}: this is Obligation 1 of \cref{sec:intro}, discharged locally by the per-production obligations of \cref{sec:per-production}.
Moreover, since every production of \cref{sec:grammar} is \emph{pol-explainable} (each \(\delta\) has a \(\den(\delta)\) that tracks its pol-level effect), we can echo to the operator, at every step of the sequence, the \texttt{pol} that the live control actually realizes.
The chain of \(\den\)s starting from \(\lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor\) gives \(\lfloor \mathtt{link}_i \rfloor\) for each \texttt{i}, so the operator is never in the dark about what policy is running.

\paragraph{How operators author sequences}
Two authoring modes produce sequences, and the operator chooses freely between them.
In \emph{declarative mode}, the operator writes a \texttt{pol}, say \texttt{p1}, and, to reconfigure, writes a second \texttt{pol}, say \texttt{p2}: the planner (\cref{sec:planner}) proposes a guarded sequence that achieves this change, and the operator either accepts it (in which case we apply the sequence to the running control) or declines it.
The planner is intentionally simple: it sees only \texttt{pol}-level differences whose translation to a sequence is straightforward, and falls back to a whole-tree \Replace{} idiom for anything richer.
In \emph{imperative mode}, the operator writes both their desired \texttt{p2} and a \((\varphi; \delta)^{*}\) sequence intended to reach it; the sequence may include idioms.
Before running it we check the sequence at the pol level (\cref{sec:idioms}).
The two modes are not formally distinct: they produce sequences over the same substrate and discharge the same per-production obligations from \cref{sec:per-production}.
Imperative mode buys expressivity, not a different proof obligation.

\paragraph{Follow-up requests}
The transition planner engages with one reconfiguration at a time.
When the operator submits a follow-up \texttt{p3} while a \(\mathtt{p1} \to \mathtt{p2}\) sequence is still mid-flight (some guard \(\varphi_i\) has not yet become true), we queue \texttt{p3}, and the planner does not begin work on it until the in-flight sequence completes and the live control \(C_z\) satisfies \(\lfloor C_z \rfloor \eqR \mathtt{p2}\).
At that instant the planner pulls \texttt{p3} from the queue, treats \texttt{p2'} as the new starting point, and produces a fresh sequence to reach \texttt{p3}.
A more aggressive strategy is possible: splicing or canceling the in-flight sequence to converge on \texttt{p3} directly, sometimes at lower total cost than running \(\mathtt{p1} \to \mathtt{p2} \to \mathtt{p3}\) in series.
We do not pursue this here; the splicing analysis introduces its own correctness obligations (atomicity of the splice, what \texttt{link} the operator observes between the splice and \texttt{p3}) that are out of scope of this paper.

\subsection{Guarded Sequences and Their Idioms}\label{sec:idioms}

\paragraph{Grammar}

\begin{verbatim}
phi    ::= true
         | empty(path)

gseq   ::= eps
         | (phi ; d) ; gseq
\end{verbatim}

We write \texttt{gseq} for ``guarded sequence.''
A guard \(\varphi\) is either trivially satisfied (\texttt{true}) or asserts that the subtree at \texttt{path} is empty; it is always paired with exactly one \(\delta\) drawn from \cref{sec:grammar}.
The two guard forms have sufficed for every sequence we have needed; the framework does not depend on this minimality, and a richer predicate language can be slotted in without disturbing anything else.

\paragraph{Intuition}

The hardware substrate (\cref{sec:hardware}) does two things beyond ordinary \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop}: it waits for a guard \(\varphi\) to become true on the live control, and it fires a single \(\delta\) as an atomic replacement.
A guarded sequence chains these: wait for \(\varphi_0\), fire \(\delta_0\), then wait for \(\varphi_1\) (now evaluated on the post-\(\delta_0\) control), fire \(\delta_1\), and so on.
Each intermediate control between two consecutive \(\delta\) firings is an ordinary \cref{sec:runtime-model} control, so the ``transitionary period'' has no new semantics; safety is entirely local, following from the per-production obligations of \cref{sec:per-production}.\footnote{Liveness is a separate concern that this paper does not chase. A planner that emits an unsatisfiable guard, or an imperative-mode operator whose sequence adversarial traffic will never satisfy, will stall; the live control remains safe but never reaches the target. Follow-up requests during a sequence are queued (see \cref{sec:sequences}'s intro).}

\paragraph{Idioms}

\emph{Idioms} are an operator-facing vocabulary for frequently-used guarded sequences, and are the typical building block of imperative-mode sequences (\cref{sec:sequences}'s intro).

An idiom is a macro over the grammar \(\delta\) (and, recursively, over other idioms).
It expands into a fixed \((\varphi; \delta)^{*}\) sequence: a list of productions with the guards between them spelling out what the system waits for.
Soundness is compositional: each step of the expansion is sound by \cref{sec:per-production}, and the sequence inherits the sequence-level reasoning above.
An idiom expansion that would hit an undefined production on the current control is rejected: the soundness checks fire on the expanded sequence just as they would on a hand-written one.

We name four starter idioms.
New ones can be added later without changing the framework, since an idiom is just a named \((\varphi; \delta)^{*}\).

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\Retire(\texttt{path})} expands to
\[
(\mathtt{true}; \Quiesce(\mathtt{path})) ; (\mathtt{empty}(\mathtt{path}); \Remove(\mathtt{path})).
\]
Quiesces the subtree at \texttt{path}, waits for it to drain to empty, then \Remove{}s it.
The operator-facing way to say ``tear this subtree down gracefully.''

\item \textbf{\SlowRetire(\texttt{path})} = \((\mathtt{empty}(\mathtt{path}); \Remove(\mathtt{path}))\).
Waits for the subtree at \texttt{path} to drain, then \Remove{}s it.
The operator may use this if the subtree at \texttt{path} needs to receive a little more traffic but is generally being phased out.

\item \textbf{\Replace(\texttt{path, B})} =

\begin{verbatim}
(true; Designate(path, B)) ;
(true; Quiesce(path ++ [0])) ;
(empty(path ++ [0]); Undesignate(path))
\end{verbatim}

Designates \texttt{B} as the survivor of the current \texttt{pol@path} (which, after Designate, sits at \texttt{path ++ [0]} as the first arm of the inserted \(\Strictstar\) node), quiesces it, waits for it to drain, then collapses the \(\Strictstar\) onto \texttt{B}.
At the pol level the composite effect is wholesale replacement of \texttt{pol@path} by \texttt{B}; the operator-facing idiom adds the \Quiesce{} in the middle so that the original subtree empties out before the collapse fires.
The footprint of \Replace(\texttt{path, B}) is the subtree at \texttt{path}, the wrap node inserted at \texttt{path} by \Designate, and the \texttt{z} chain at the ancestors of \texttt{path} (extended by \Designate, restored by \Undesignate); \cref{sec:planner} frames confinement against exactly this set.

\item \textbf{\PruneDownTo(\texttt{path})} expands to
\[
\Retire(\pi_a) ; \ldots ; \Retire(\pi_z) ; (\mathtt{true}; \ChangeRoot(\mathtt{path})),
\]
where \(\pi_a, \ldots, \pi_z\) are paths to the off-path subtrees along the route from the root to \texttt{path}.
This idiom is the operator-facing way to say ``abandon everything except this subtree.''

Each \Retire{} removes one off-path subtree; once they have all fired, every ancestor along the path is unary, satisfying \ChangeRoot's precondition (\cref{sec:changeroot}).
(If no off-path subtrees exist, the idiom reduces to \((\mathtt{true}; \ChangeRoot(\mathtt{path}))\) alone.)
The \Retire{}s on disjoint off-path subtrees commute, so any ordering suffices; we use root-first as a stable convention.

For instance, suppose the running tree is \texttt{A(B(E(F, G), D), C)}:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
  level distance=0.9cm,
  level 1/.style={sibling distance=2.4cm},
  level 2/.style={sibling distance=1.4cm},
  level 3/.style={sibling distance=0.9cm},
  every node/.style={font=\small}
]
\node {$A$}
  child {node {$B$}
    child {node {$E$}
      child {node {$F$}}
      child {node {$G$}}
    }
    child {node {$D$}}
  }
  child {node {$C$}};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The index paths to its nodes are \texttt{A} at \texttt{[]}, \texttt{B} at \texttt{[0]}, \texttt{C} at \texttt{[1]}, \texttt{E(F, G)} at \texttt{[0, 0]}, \texttt{F} at \texttt{[0, 0, 0]}, \texttt{G} at \texttt{[0, 0, 1]}, and \texttt{D} at \texttt{[0, 1]}.

The operator wants to retain only \texttt{D}, so they issue \PruneDownTo(\texttt{[0, 1]}).

The off-path subtrees are \texttt{E(F, G)} (at \texttt{[0, 0]}) and \texttt{C} (at \texttt{[1]}).
So \PruneDownTo(\texttt{[0, 1]}) expands to: \Retire(\texttt{[0, 0]) ; Retire([1]) ; (true; ChangeRoot([0, 0])}).

Note that the path \texttt{[0, 0]} appears twice in this expansion with different referents: in \Retire(\texttt{[0, 0]}) it points to \texttt{E(F, G)} (the operand at the moment that \Retire{} fires), and in the final \ChangeRoot(\texttt{[0, 0]}) it points to \texttt{D}, which moved from \texttt{[0, 1]} to \texttt{[0, 0]} once \texttt{E(F, G)} was retired.
A path-resolution system computes these targets automatically at idiom expansion; the operator only writes \PruneDownTo(\texttt{[0, 1]}), naming \texttt{D} by its location at the moment of request.

\emph{On the natural-feeling opposite.}
\PruneDownTo{} says ``keep this subtree and abandon everything around it''; the natural-feeling opposite would say ``keep the running tree and splice some fresh ancestor structure around it'' (the running root becoming a subtree of a freshly-introduced context).
We do not support this as a production or as an idiom.
The obstruction is not topological but stateful: a freshly-spawned ancestor needs a \texttt{node\_state}, per-arm \texttt{slot\_state}s, a populated index \texttt{pifo}, and a \texttt{z} whose history matches the live traffic descending into the subtree it now wraps, and there is no honest way to synthesize the state these ancestors would have carried \emph{had they been there all along}.
Disciplines that look at packet features or history (WFQ's virtual time, RR's cursor, LSTF's per-deadline ranks) make this concrete: an ancestor introduced cold would mis-schedule the in-flight traffic relative to what an ancestor present from the start would have done.
The operator can still reach such a target through a \Replace{} at a sufficient ancestor (\cref{sec:fallback}'s always-available fallback), which freshly compiles the new wrapping and drains the old running subtree through it; what the grammar lacks is a confined way to install the wrap without that drain.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Checking an imperative-mode sequence at the pol level}

Because imperative-mode sequences (\cref{sec:sequences}'s intro) are user-authored, we validate them before commit.
The check folds each production's \(\den(\delta_i)\) (\cref{sec:per-production}) along the sequence, producing the intermediate pols \(\mathtt{ip}_0 = \mathtt{p1'}, \mathtt{ip}_1, \ldots, \mathtt{ip}_{n+1}\) with \(\mathtt{ip}_{i+1} := \den(\delta_i)(\mathtt{ip}_i)\), where \(\mathtt{p1'} = \lfloor \mathtt{C} \rfloor\) is the pol we echoed to the operator.
We accept the request iff every \(\den(\delta_i)\) is defined on \(\mathtt{ip}_i\) and \(\mathtt{ip}_{n+1} \eqR \mathtt{p2}\).
That is, the user-provided sequence actually takes \texttt{p1'} to \texttt{p2}.
This is the Interplay theorem (\cref{sec:interplay}) iterated across the sequence: each step's \(\den(\delta_i)\) matches its operational counterpart \(\sem{\delta_i}\) per-production in \cref{sec:per-production}, so a fold that lands at \(\mathtt{ip}_{n+1} \eqR \mathtt{p2}\) certifies, cell-by-cell, that the operational chain ends in a control whose \(\lfloor \cdot \rfloor\) is \texttt{p2}.

Each \(\mathtt{ip}_i\) is itself a valid pol with an explainable scheduling semantics, not a transient artifact of the proof.
For instance, after the first step of \Replace(\texttt{path, B})'s expansion the intermediate pol holds \(\Strict(\mathtt{pol@path}, B)\) at \texttt{path}: a temporary strict-priority node favoring the outgoing \texttt{pol@path} over the incoming \texttt{B}, with a readable scheduling interpretation in its own right.

The ``defined when'' clauses on each \(\den(\delta_i)\) are the per-production preconditions listed in \crefrange{sec:add}{sec:changeroot}.
Guards play no role in this check; they govern the operational timing of when each \(\delta_i\) fires on the live control, and are pol-invisible (\cref{sec:per-production}).
For instance, a \((\mathtt{empty}(\mathtt{path}); \Remove(\mathtt{path}))\) step folds at the pol level exactly as if its guard were \texttt{true}, since \(\den\) only sees \(\delta\).
If the chain fails to reach \texttt{p2}, or some \(\den(\delta_i)\) is undefined on its intermediate pol, we reject the request.
The rejection identifies the offending step: the first \(\delta_i\) whose \(\den(\delta_i)\) was undefined on \(\mathtt{ip}_i\), or, if every step was defined, the final mismatch \(\mathtt{ip}_{n+1} \not\eqR \mathtt{p2}\).

\section{The Transition Planner}\label{sec:planner}

\cref{sec:sequences} referenced a \emph{transition planner}; this section spells it out.
The operator hands the runtime \texttt{p1} (the presently-running policy) and \texttt{p2} (their new desire); the planner's job is to propose a \((\varphi ; \delta)^{*}\) sequence whose pol-level fold takes \texttt{p1} to \texttt{p2} up to \(\eqR\).
The operator accepts or rejects the proposal; they do not author it directly.

Every sequence the planner emits is safe by the per-production guarantees of \cref{sec:per-production}, aggregated across the sequence as sketched in \cref{sec:idioms}, regardless of how the planner assembled it.
The planner aims to land at \texttt{p2} by construction: each case below emits a \(\delta\) or idiom whose \(\den\) realizes the locally-observed pol-level difference.
A misstep in the case analysis could in principle yield a sequence whose fold \(\den(\delta_n) \circ \ldots \circ \den(\delta_1) (\mathtt{p1})\) misses \texttt{p2}'s \(\eqR\)-class; the pol-level check of \cref{sec:idioms} is the backstop and rejects any such sequence before commit.
Optimality is a separate matter, and not guaranteed: the planner may fail to see a superior sequence that the user has in mind.

\cref{sec:fallback} sets up \emph{confinement} as a metric for excellence, presents the always-available worst-case \Replace{} fallback, and frames the planner's strategy as descending the tree to find a tight production (Add, Remove, ChangeMeta) that captures the difference locally.
\cref{sec:planner-emits} catalogs the cases the planner recognizes and emits a tight sequence for.
\cref{sec:planner-nonfeatures} lists deliberate non-features and candidate future work.

\subsection{The Always-Available Fallback, and Confinement to do Better}\label{sec:fallback}

To reach any \texttt{p2} from any \texttt{p1}, the planner can issue \(\Replace([], \mathtt{p2})\), the \cref{sec:idioms} idiom that expands to

\begin{verbatim}
(true ; Designate([], p2)) ;
(true ; Quiesce([0])) ;
(empty([0]) ; Undesignate([]))
\end{verbatim}

After \(\Designate([], \mathtt{p2})\) fires, the original \texttt{p1} sits at child index 0 of the freshly-introduced \(\Strictstar\) wrap and the survivor \texttt{p2} sits at index 1 (\cref{sec:designate}); the \texttt{[0]} in the next two steps targets the \texttt{p1} arm.
This wraps \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2} into a \(\Strictstar(\mathtt{hi}: \mathtt{p1}, \mathtt{lo}: \mathtt{p2})\) at the root, quiesces the \texttt{p1} arm, accepts pushes into \texttt{p2} and serves pops from \texttt{p1}, and, when \texttt{p1} has drained to empty, replaces \(\Strictstar(\mathtt{hi}: \mathtt{p1}, \mathtt{lo}: \mathtt{p2})\) with just \texttt{p2}, which then serves both pushes and pops.
Nothing is dropped and the sequence is safe by \cref{sec:per-production}, but \emph{no part of the scheduler is left undisturbed}.

\paragraph{Footprint}

To measure how much of the tree a sequence touches, associate each production \(\delta\) with a \emph{footprint} on the live control it acts on.
Define \(\mathtt{footprint}(\delta, \mathtt{C})\) as the set of nodes of \texttt{C} whose \texttt{state}, \texttt{pifo}, or \texttt{z} differs between \texttt{C} and \(\sem{\delta}(\mathtt{C})\).
The set is read off the per-production Preservation paragraphs of \cref{sec:per-production}: it includes the edit site, the new or removed node where applicable, and every proper ancestor whose \texttt{z} is extended or restricted (e.g., the ancestors touched by \Add, \Quiesce, \Remove, \Designate).
For a guarded sequence \((\varphi_0 ; \delta_0) ; \ldots ; (\varphi_n ; \delta_n)\), the sequence-level footprint is the union \(\bigcup_i \mathtt{footprint}(\delta_i, \mathtt{link}_i)\).
The root-level \(\Replace([], \mathtt{p2})\) above has the maximum possible footprint: the whole tree.

\paragraph{The planner's strategy}

The metric by which we judge the planner is \emph{confinement}: a good emission disturbs as little of the running scheduler as possible, leaving parts of the tree that did not need to change running undisturbed.
A sequence is well-confined when its footprint is small relative to the symmetric pol-level difference between \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2}.

The planner's goal is to recognize whether the difference between \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2} is captured by a tight production from \cref{sec:delta-grammar} (an \Add, \Remove, or \ChangeMeta) and to emit that production wherever in the tree it sits, leaving everything else untouched.
The planner descends through the tree as long as \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2} agree at the current level, looking for a recognized shape one level down.
When it finds one, it emits the matching production at that depth.
Only when the descent ends without a match does the planner fall back to \Replace; but by then the recursion has localized the difference to the deepest slot at which the policies disagree, so the resulting \Replace{} wraps only that slot.

A concrete example will help.
Suppose

\]
and the operator requests
\[

\]

\subsection{What the planner emits}\label{sec:planner-emits}

The planner has two parts.
The top-level pass examines \((\mathtt{p1}, \mathtt{p2})\) as whole trees and one of three things happens: it finds them equal up to \(\eqR\) and emits \texttt{[]}, or it recognizes that \texttt{p2} already lives somewhere inside \texttt{p1} and therefore emits a \PruneDownTo, or it passes the pair to a second procedure that compares two policies at one level (which we will call the \emph{per-level comparator}).
The per-level comparator either recognizes a tight production at the current level, or recurses into matching slots, or gives up and emits a slot-level \Replace.
Whatever the per-level comparator emits is lifted back to the right depth by prepending the slot indices that the recursion descended through.

\paragraph{The top-level pass}

The pass tries three cases in order and stops at the first match.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{\(\mathtt{p1} = \mathtt{p2}\).} Emit \texttt{[]}.
The test is literal equality on the normalized \texttt{pol} terms.
The compiler (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}) runs every operator-supplied policy through \texttt{Pol.normalize} before reaching the planner, so two \(\eqR\)-equivalent policies arrive here as identical terms.
The live control is already serving the requested policy and there is nothing to install.

\item \textbf{\texttt{p2} embeds in \texttt{p1} at \texttt{path}.} Emit \(\PruneDownTo(\mathtt{path})\).
A structural sub-policy test walks \texttt{p1} looking for a subtree equal to \texttt{p2}; if it finds one, \texttt{path} names where it sits.
The match is unique: by \cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s leaf-partition validity each flow in \texttt{p2} may appear at most once in \texttt{p1}, so \texttt{p1} can host \texttt{p2} as a subtree in at most one position.
This test runs only at the top level.

\item \textbf{Otherwise.} Hand \((\mathtt{p1}, \mathtt{p2})\) to the per-level comparator.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The per-level comparator}

The per-level comparator looks at the roots of \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2}.
If the two roots use different constructors (for example \texttt{SP} and \texttt{RR}, or two \texttt{FIFO}s with different classes), it emits a slot-level \Replace{} directly: the always-available \cref{sec:fallback} idiom restricted to the current slot.
If the constructors agree, it then looks at the two child lists and compares their lengths.

\textbf{Equal arity.}
For the constructors that carry per-arm metas (\texttt{SP} and \texttt{WFQ}), we first check for a meta-only reshuffle: if \texttt{p1} and \texttt{p2} hold the same multiset of arms in different positions, we emit one \ChangeMeta{} per slot whose meta moved and we are done.

Otherwise, we walk the child lists slot-by-slot.
A slot whose arm and meta both agree contributes nothing.
A slot whose meta differs but whose arm agrees contributes a \(\ChangeMeta(\mathtt{path}, \mathtt{new\_meta})\).
A slot whose arm differs is handled by recursing to try and narrow down the difference deeper in the tree; if the meta also differs, we append a follow-on \((\mathtt{true}; \ChangeMeta(\mathtt{path}, \mathtt{new\_meta}))\) after the inner emission.

There is one wrinkle: this slot-by-slot walk can miss a cross-slot correspondence in which an arm has moved to a different slot in \texttt{p2} \emph{and} also changed structurally.
The label-overlap walk (below) catches it; when that walk finds at least one such cross-slot pairing, we use it instead of the slot-by-slot walk.

\textbf{\texttt{p2} longer.}
When \texttt{p1}'s arms appear in order as a sub-sequence of \texttt{p2}'s, the surplus arms in \texttt{p2} are pure additions, and we emit one \Add{} per surplus arm in ascending index order.
\cref{sec:add}'s leaf-disjointness precondition holds automatically: every surplus arm's leaves are leaves of \texttt{p2} not in \texttt{p1}, which by \cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s leaf-partition validity are disjoint from \texttt{p1}'s arms and from each other.
At an \texttt{SP} or \texttt{WFQ} parent, each \Add{} carries the surplus arm's meta.
If a stable arm has changed metadata, we emit \ChangeMeta{}s after completing the \Add{}s.

When \texttt{p1}'s arms are \emph{not} a sub-sequence of \texttt{p2}'s, we try the label-overlap walk.
If that fails too, we emit a slot-level \Replace.

\textbf{\texttt{p1} longer.}
Symmetric.
When \texttt{p2}'s arms appear in order as a sub-sequence of \texttt{p1}'s, the surplus arms in \texttt{p1} are retired, and we emit one \Retire{} idiom (\cref{sec:idioms}) per surplus arm in \emph{descending} index order.
Descending order keeps the lower-index paths stable while higher-index siblings drain and disappear.
When \texttt{p2}'s arms are not a sub-sequence of \texttt{p1}'s, we try the label-overlap walk, and emit a slot-level \Replace{} if it cannot align the arms either.

\textbf{The label-overlap walk.}
The simpler patterns above pair arms by slot position; the label-overlap walk pairs them by what they actually carry.
Consider the following example.

\begin{verbatim}

p2 = RR(drive, slack, RR(gmail, controller_for_gmail))
\end{verbatim}

The installed tree previews what lands on the substrate: not \texttt{p2} literally, but a sibling-reordered representative of its \(\eqR\)-class; we return to it below.

A slot-by-slot walk does not apply (the lengths differ), and the simpler sub-sequence check in the ``\texttt{p2} longer'' case fails because \texttt{gmail} in \texttt{p1} is not equal to any arm in \texttt{p2}.
Without the walk we are about to describe, the planner would give up and emit a wholesale \Replace{} of the entire root \texttt{RR}.

But the trees are not really that different.
\texttt{gmail} in \texttt{p1} and \texttt{RR(gmail, gmail\_control)} in \texttt{p2} share the class label \texttt{gmail}; \texttt{drive} is the same on both sides; \texttt{slack} is the only arm in \texttt{p2} whose labels appear nowhere in \texttt{p1}.
The correct reading is that \texttt{drive} stays, \texttt{gmail} has grown a sub-channel, and \texttt{slack} is fresh.

The label-overlap walk produces that reading.
It pairs arms in \texttt{p1} with arms in \texttt{p2} whose class-label sets overlap (by a greedy left-to-right walk); arms in \texttt{p1} with no overlapping partner retire, arms in \texttt{p2} with no overlapping partner are added, and matched pairs are recursed on.
The justification is that a class label identifies a unique flow across the whole policy (\cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s leaf-partition validity), so a repeated label between an arm in \texttt{p1} and an arm in \texttt{p2} is evidence that the same host arm has morphed structurally, and an arm whose labels appear on no opposing arm is necessarily fresh or vanishing.
For our example, the walk pairs \texttt{drive} with \texttt{drive} (no edit) and \texttt{gmail} with \texttt{RR(gmail, gmail\_control)} (recurse), and treats \texttt{slack} as a pure add.

Our final emission is two atomic steps: an \Add{} of \texttt{slack} appended at the root \texttt{RR}'s tail, and an inner \Replace{} at \texttt{gmail}'s existing slot.
The third panel of the figure above shows the resulting substrate state: \texttt{slack} sits beside the morphed \texttt{gmail} subtree in an order that does not match \texttt{p2} literally, but \cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s normalization invariant says sibling order at \texttt{SP}/\texttt{RR}/\texttt{WFQ} carries no semantics, so the re-ordering is invisible to any observer.

A few notes.
The walk is greedy: when more than one alignment satisfies the label-overlap constraint, the first we find is taken, with no notion of cost (\cref{sec:planner-nonfeatures}).
If no shared label admits even one match, the walk fails and the caller falls back to a slot-level \Replace.
When the walk produces a mix of adds, retires, and matched-pair recursions, we appeal to the reorder freedom of \cref{sec:policy-dsl} to keep the indexing trivial: adds are appended at the parent's tail; retires fire at their ps1 indices, in descending order so the highest-index drain doesn't shift the lower indices still pending; matched-pair recursions land at each match's position in the matches list, which is also its slot once the retires drain.
Issuing the adds first is deliberate: an \Add{} is instantaneous while a \Retire{} waits for its subtree to drain, so the new arms start carrying traffic immediately instead of waiting out the drains.

\paragraph{Where the descent localizes the difference}

Every recursion descends through a slot where the two trees agree or where the label-overlap walk has paired them.
So by the time the per-level comparator gives up and emits a \Replace{} at depth \texttt{k}, every shallower level has already matched a tight case, and the \Replace{} lands at the deepest slot to which the planner could isolate the difference, leaving every ancestor's other subtrees running undisturbed.
The slot-level \Replace{} is the \cref{sec:fallback} idiom with a non-empty path: it wraps only the slot's subtree, quiesces only that subtree, and rewires only the parent edge into it.
The worst case is the root-level \(\Replace([], \mathtt{p2})\) of \cref{sec:fallback}, reached only when the per-level comparator gives up at the root with no recursive descent.

We do not claim that the planner's emission is canonical or minimal.
We claim only that it is safe (\cref{sec:per-production}) and no worse than the root-level fallback.

\subsection{What the planner does not do today}\label{sec:planner-nonfeatures}

Two non-features for now; we hope to improve these in the next few meetings.
\emph{No cost model:} the case analysis is shape-based, not cost-weighted.
A cost-aware planner could prefer different decompositions (e.g., several small \Add{}s vs.\ a single root-level \Replace) based on per-flow drop and delay tolerances or per-PE budgets.
Presently we take the first admissible alignment without ranking competitors.
\emph{No imperative-mode service:} the planner serves declarative mode only.
Imperative-mode authoring (\cref{sec:sequences}'s intro) goes through the pol-level check of \cref{sec:idioms} directly, with the operator naming each \(\delta\) themselves; the planner is not consulted.

\section{Rio Hardware Implementation}\label{sec:hardware}

A PIFO tree is a clean scheduling abstraction, but instantiating one dynamically on line-rate switching hardware needs more than a fixed set of hardwired PIFOs.
Existing designs~\cite{TODO} make a logical-to-physical unit mapping to materialize programmable scheduling: instead of hard-wiring a fixed set of PIFOs into hardware, the switch exposes programmable processing elements (PEs) hosting multiple virtual PIFOs.
It includes hardware (usually match-action pipelines) that maps packets to its virtual PIFOs and calculates the rank and next-hop destination, followed by one physical PIFO hosting all of the virtual PIFOs on the PE.

Packet scheduling on this substrate consists of pipelined, non-atomic enqueue and dequeue operations.
On enqueue, the switch must compute two pieces of information before issuing PE pushes: which virtual PIFO id the packet should enter, and what rank should be inserted at each of those virtual PIFOs.
The first is determined by packet classification, flow-to-child mappings; the second is computed by the virtual PIFO's scheduling policy from packet fields, flow state, and per-arm metadata.
For example, a WFQ-style virtual PIFO uses the child weight to compute a virtual finish time, while a Strict virtual PIFO interprets the metadata as a priority rank.
On dequeue, the switch starts from the output port's root virtual PIFO, pops the best-ranked entry, and follows the stored next-hop information until it reaches a packet to transmit or another virtual PIFO to pop.

This is sufficient for a static policy, but it does not by itself support dynamic policy updates in concurrent with packet enqueue and dequeue.
In such a case, a single packet operation can observe a mixture of old and new scheduler state.
For example, an enqueue may choose its virtual PIFO path using the old flow-to-child mapping but compute its rank using newly updated per-arm metadata, or a dequeue may pop from one version of the tree and then follow next-hop state from another.
As a result, existing PIFO-based substrates support only stop-the-world policy update.

\subsection{Transactional Update and Hardware Component Support}\label{sec:hw-transactional}

\emph{Vocabulary bridge to \S6.1' (temporary, until the two subsections are reconciled).}
The ``flow-to-child mapping'', ``next-hop mapping'', and ``match-action tables'' of this subsection are the tables that \S6.1''s \texttt{map} / \texttt{assoc} / \texttt{adopt} opcodes write to; the ``virtual PIFOs'' of this subsection are the PIFOs of \S6.1', one per PIFO-tree node.

Rio enables online policy update by packaging multiple hardware state updates into a single ``commit'' and making them visible atomically in hardware: every enqueue or dequeue request observes either the whole commit applied, or none of it.
Each \(\delta\) (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) lowers to one \texttt{commit} under this abstraction.

In Rio hardware, we achieve this by packaging multiple policy updates into a single ``transaction'' and making minimal hardware changes in virtual PIFO, rank, and next-hop mapping to enable transactional commit of multiple updates.

First, Rio builds transactional mapping tables for virtual PIFO and next-hop mapping.
Each PE stores its flow-to-child mapping and next-hop state in a specialized table that accepts a stream of update instructions, and a commit signal.
Updates before the signal are staged but remain invisible to packet processing.
Only when the commit marker is applied does the table immediately switch to the new version.
We implement this using double buffering: update instructions modify a shadow copy of the table, and the commit marker atomically swaps the active and shadow copies.
After the swap, the inactive copy is synchronized in the background to serve as the shadow for the next commit.

Second, Rio supports incremental updates to the rank-computing match-action tables.
Rio does not require arbitrary in-place replacement of a live scheduling policy.
Instead, policy-table updates correspond to adding metadata for newly admitted flows (whose packets are not allowed before the update is finished) or removing metadata for flows that have already been drained.
Because these updates do not change the interpretation of existing live packets, Rio applies them incrementally rather than through a full transactional table swap.
This keeps the hardware cost low while still preserving the atomicity needed for observable scheduling behavior.

\subsection*{6.1' The commit model and the ISA}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{6.1' The commit model and the ISA}

\emph{Vocabulary bridge from \cref{sec:hw-transactional} (temporary, until the two subsections are reconciled).}
\cref{sec:hw-transactional} speaks in ``flow-to-child mapping'', ``next-hop mapping'', ``match-action tables'', and ``virtual PIFOs''.
This subsection speaks in \texttt{map} / \texttt{assoc} (which write the flow-to-child mapping), \texttt{adopt} / \texttt{emancipate} (which install and detach parent/child edges, i.e., the next-hop mapping), the per-PIFO tables that these opcodes write to (\cref{sec:hw-transactional}'s match-action tables), and PIFOs (which correspond to \cref{sec:hw-transactional}'s virtual PIFOs, one per PIFO-tree node).

The substrate exposes its services to the planner as an atomic-commit interface.
The ISA has exactly one unit: a \texttt{commit}, which is a list of instructions that the substrate installs \emph{atomically}.
The entire list lands between two consecutive \texttt{push}/\texttt{pop} operations, with no intermediate state visible to the user's \texttt{pop} stream.
This is precisely the atomicity that \cref{sec:per-production}'s \emph{Preservation of observation} obligation demanded.
A commit's \emph{length} may depend on live control state (how many flows are admitted, how many siblings need shifting), but its \emph{atomicity} does not.

Each \(\delta\) (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) lowers to one \texttt{commit}; that is the entire ISA-level contract.
The ISA itself is unconditional: each instruction describes what to do, not what to check.
The substrate trusts that its caller has issued a well-formed commit and acts accordingly.
Any situation-dependent reasoning (e.g., ``the subtree we are about to collapse is empty'') lives at \cref{sec:sequences}, in a guard \(\varphi\) that gated the firing of the corresponding \(\delta\), not in the substrate.

Four pieces of substrate vocabulary appear in the listing below:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{PIFO}: one node of the PIFO-tree, addressed by an opaque id \texttt{v}.
The substrate exposes one PIFO per tree node; how those PIFOs are mapped onto hardware is a substrate concern (see ``PE deployment'' later in this section).
\item \textbf{PE} (processing element): hosts one or more PIFOs and owns their per-node state and logic.
A single PE can host multiple PIFOs at once; in particular, sibling PIFOs commonly cohabit a PE.
\item \textbf{flow}: a traffic class.
\item \textbf{index}: opaque per-PIFO handle naming one of that PIFO's children; what a PIFO uses internally to refer to a child arm.
\end{itemize}

The ISA has thirteen opcodes, laid out in \cref{tab:isa-opcodes}.

Our single-purpose opcodes also mean that the tree can drift transiently malformed \emph{within} a commit, in ways that the commit's atomicity hides from any observer.
For an instance from \cref{sec:hw-compile}: \Designate's commit first adopts \texttt{loser} as a child of the freshly-spawned \texttt{sp\_v}, then emancipates \texttt{loser} from its original parent and adopts \texttt{sp\_v} in its place.
Between the first adopt and the emancipate, \texttt{loser} has two parents.
The atomic install means no \texttt{push} or \texttt{pop} ever sees this state, but the planner is free to issue commits whose intermediate frames would not satisfy the \cref{sec:runtime-model} well-formedness invariants.

One last piece of setup the lowerings in \cref{sec:hw-compile} will lean on: \emph{PE deployment}.
We treat the assignment of a PIFO to a PE as a deterministic function \texttt{pe(path)} of the PIFO's tree position, fixed at compile time.
A typical shape is depth-by-PE: PIFOs at the same tree depth share a PE~\cite{Sivaraman16}, so sibling PIFOs cohabit one PE by default.
The planner consults \texttt{pe(path)} when it needs a \texttt{spawn}'s \texttt{pe} argument; the function's definition is a property of the target substrate and is not otherwise observable at the ISA.

\subsection{Compiling Policy Differences}\label{sec:hw-compile}

Each production of \(\delta\) (\cref{sec:delta-grammar}) lowers to a list of op codes.
The lowering \emph{schema} is mechanical: each production names a fixed sequence of opcodes, and the planner reads live state at issue time to instantiate the schema's positional parameters (flow lists, chain shapes, routing indices) before dispatching the commit to the substrate.

Before working through the productions, we set up one structural convention.
A switch with \(P\) output ports has \(P\) separate trees, and at the ISA we put a uniform thin wrapper around each.
Every port hosts a reserved PIFO \texttt{port\_root}, allocated at boot on a reserved PE, whose sole child is the actual tree root.
\texttt{port\_root} runs a fixed 1-arm policy.
Its \texttt{Assoc} set mirrors that of the live tree's actual root; its \texttt{Map} sends each Assoc'd flow through its single index \texttt{port\_step}.
This \texttt{port\_root} state is load-bearing for \ChangeRoot, and it is maintained by two complementary mechanisms.
On every production except \ChangeRoot, the \texttt{walk(...)} calls that touch \texttt{Assoc}/\texttt{Map} start at \texttt{port\_root} and so update its tables in lockstep with the actual root's.
On \ChangeRoot, the lowering issues no flow-table writes against \texttt{port\_root} at all: the swap is exactly one \texttt{emancipate}/\texttt{adopt} pair against \texttt{port\_root}, and the existing \texttt{Assoc}/\texttt{Map} state survives the swap because \texttt{port\_step} is the index \emph{name}, unbound to any particular child.
The wrapper exists so that ``swap the root'' can be expressed as one \texttt{emancipate}/\texttt{adopt} pair against \texttt{port\_root}, in the same vocabulary as any child-level edit; there is no opcode for changing the root of the tree directly.

The lowerings follow, one per production in the order of the \cref{sec:delta-grammar} grammar.
We fix a handful of reading conventions.
We identify a tree position with the PIFO that lives there.
Live queries against a node \texttt{v} (its \texttt{arity(v)}, \texttt{discipline(v)}, etc.) are read off the tree at issue time.
When an opcode names an unmentioned parameter at \texttt{v} (e.g., \texttt{set\_policy}'s discipline, \texttt{change\_arity}'s arity), it is the live value at \texttt{v}.
\texttt{spawn}'s \texttt{pe} argument is similarly elided: it is \texttt{pe(pos(v))}, drawn from \S6.1''s deployment convention.

Alongside the opcodes, the listings use a small codegen vocabulary:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{foreach x in S: BODY} emits \texttt{BODY} once per \texttt{x} drawn from \texttt{S}.
\item \texttt{walk(op, C, f)} emits \texttt{op(v, f, \ldots)} at each \(v \in C\); positional remainders such as \texttt{map}'s routing index \(i_{v,f}\) are read off the live tree.
\item \texttt{flows(path)} is the set of flows admitted by some leaf under \texttt{path}.
\item \texttt{chain(f)} is the sequence of PIFOs from \texttt{port\_root} to the leaf admitting \texttt{f}, and \texttt{internals(f)} is that sequence minus the leaf.
\item \texttt{nodes(T)} and \texttt{edges(T)} are the PIFOs and parent-child edges of a compiled subtree \texttt{T}; \texttt{root(T)} is \texttt{T}'s root PIFO.
\item \texttt{layout(T)} expands to \texttt{spawn}+\texttt{set\_policy} at every \(v \in \mathtt{nodes(T)}\), \texttt{adopt} at every internal edge in \(\mathtt{edges(T)}\), and \texttt{set\_arm\_meta} at each per-arm slot where \texttt{v}'s discipline requires it.
The parent-side \texttt{adopt} that attaches \(\mathtt{root(T)}\) is left to the caller.
\end{itemize}

Where a production destructures \(\delta\)'s path as \(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k]\), we write \texttt{p} for the parent's path and \texttt{k} for the child's local index.
For \texttt{path = []}, the parent is \texttt{port\_root} and \texttt{k} is \texttt{port\_step}.

\paragraph*{\(\ChangeMeta(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k], m)\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
set_arm_meta(p, k, m)
\end{Verbatim}

\paragraph*{\(\Quiesce(\mathtt{path})\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
foreach f in flows(path):
    walk(deassoc, chain(f), f)
\end{Verbatim}

\paragraph*{\(\Add(\mathtt{path}, \mathtt{arm}, \mathtt{meta?})\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
n := arity(path)
layout(arm)
change_arity(path, n+1)
if meta?: set_arm_meta(path, n, meta?)
adopt(n, path, root(arm))
foreach f in flows(arm):
    walk(assoc, chain(f), f)
    walk(map,   internals(f), f)
\end{Verbatim}
The new subtree occupies \texttt{path}'s rightmost slot (index \texttt{n}), so existing children's indices are undisturbed.

\paragraph*{\(\Remove(\pi \mathbin{+\!\!+} [k])\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
change_arity(p, arity(p) - 1)
emancipate(k, p)
foreach f in flows(p ++ [k]):
    walk(unmap, internals(f), f)
foreach v in nodes(p ++ [k]):
    gc(v)
\end{Verbatim}
\texttt{p}'s remaining children keep their indices: each was minted at adoption time, so no sibling-shift cascade is needed.

\paragraph*{\(\Designate(\mathtt{path}, \mathtt{survivor})\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
loser  := PIFO at path;   parent := PIFO at path's parent
k      := path's last index
surv   := root(survivor)
layout(survivor)
spawn(sp_v, pe(path))
set_policy(sp_v, Strict*, 2)
adopt(0, sp_v, loser); adopt(1, sp_v, surv)
set_arm_meta(sp_v, 0, 1.0)
set_arm_meta(sp_v, 1, 2.0)
emancipate(k, parent); adopt(k, parent, sp_v)
designate(loser, surv)
foreach f in flows(loser) \ flows(surv):
    assoc(sp_v, f); map(sp_v, f, 0)
foreach f in flows(surv):
    assoc(sp_v, f); map(sp_v, f, 1)
foreach f in flows(surv) \ flows(path):
    walk(assoc, chain(f), f)
    walk(map,   internals(f), f)
\end{Verbatim}
Reusing the same \texttt{k} for the rewire preserves \texttt{parent}'s per-arm metadata for that slot.
Flows in \(\mathtt{flows(surv)} \cap \mathtt{flows(path)}\) need no above-\texttt{sp\_v} wiring: the chain is already in place from \texttt{path}'s prior wiring.
Flows in \(\mathtt{flows(loser)} \setminus \mathtt{flows(surv)}\) keep their existing above-\texttt{sp\_v} Assoc/Map state and are routed to slot 0 by \texttt{sp\_v.z}, matching \cref{sec:designate}'s operational rule that loser-only labels drain on arm 0.
Operator-level silencing of those flows, when the operator ultimately wants it, comes from the \Quiesce{} step of the surrounding \Replace{} idiom (\cref{sec:sequences}), not from \Designate.

\emph{Same-PE invariant.}
The planner places \texttt{sp\_v}, \texttt{loser}'s root, and \texttt{surv}'s root on the same PE (a deterministic property of the lowering: all three sit at depth \(|\mathtt{path}|\), and \texttt{pe(path)} returns one PE).
The substrate may exploit this to coalesce the three into a single physical slot, sparing itself from moving all of \texttt{loser} down one PE level.

\paragraph*{\(\Undesignate(\mathtt{path})\)}
\begin{Verbatim}
sp_v    := super-node head at path
loser_v := its retiring arm
undesignate(loser_v)
gc(sp_v)
foreach v in nodes(loser_v):
    gc(v)
\end{Verbatim}
\texttt{undesignate} rewires the parent's slot past \texttt{sp\_v} but does not deallocate it, so an explicit \texttt{gc(sp\_v)} follows.

\paragraph*{\(\ChangeRoot(\mathtt{path})\)}
Let the live tree be \(\mathtt{port\_root} \to a_0 \to a_1 \to \ldots \to a_m \to \mathtt{path}\) (the \cref{sec:delta-grammar} restriction makes \(a_0 \ldots a_m\) a unary vine).
\begin{Verbatim}
emancipate(port_step, port_root)
adopt(port_step, port_root, path)
foreach a in {a_0, ..., a_m}:
    gc(a)
\end{Verbatim}

Three items in the list deserve unpacking.

\Quiesce{}'s wide reach (root-to-\texttt{path} chain, plus the subtree under \texttt{path}) is forced by \cref{sec:policy-dsl}'s parallel push: every node on a packet's path mints its own routing index independently, so dropping \texttt{f} only at some nodes would leave the rest happy to mint stray indices that would leave the tree malformed.
The chain walk is what \cref{sec:quiesce} calls \emph{restricting \texttt{z} uniformly}.
\Quiesce{} does not issue \texttt{unmap}: silenced flows' Map entries \emph{must outlive the silencing} so that buffered packets can be popped.
\Remove{} pairs \texttt{emancipate} with \texttt{unmap} precisely because it fires after the drain, when there is no longer any in-flight traffic to preserve.
One subtlety in the loser arm of an SP\(^*\) super-node (i.e., a \Quiesce{} that fires mid-\Replace{}): the lowering restricts the deassoc walk to loser-only classes, since \Designate{} routed the shared classes to the survivor's slot at commit time (\cref{sec:designate}), and deassoc'ing them here would silence live traffic on the survivor.

The lowering for \Designate{} matches \cref{sec:grammar}'s tree-level reading verbatim: a fresh Strict-2 PIFO \texttt{sp\_v} is spawned, \texttt{path}'s previous occupant becomes its favored child (rank 1.0), and the survivor becomes its second child (rank 2.0).
The lowering rewires \texttt{path}'s parent edge from \texttt{loser} to \texttt{sp\_v}, reusing the same parent-side index \texttt{k}; the parent's per-arm metadata for the slot is therefore preserved across \Designate{} and across the eventual \Undesignate{} that retires the give-up.
\texttt{designate(loser, surv)} is the ISA-level marker that names the favored child and records the eventual successor; a later \texttt{undesignate} swings the parent's index over to \texttt{surv} and releases \texttt{sp\_v} along with the loser subtree.
A substrate that recognizes the same-PE invariant on \texttt{(sp\_v, loser, surv)} is free to coalesce the three into a single physical slot (the ``super-node'' optimization); the ISA does not pin this down, leaving room for substrates that prefer the literal Strict-2 form.

\ChangeRoot{} writes no flow tables: \texttt{path}'s \texttt{Assoc} and \texttt{Map} already describe the post-commit live tree, since collapsing the unary vine above \texttt{path} leaves the flows-to-leaves mapping unchanged.

\subsection{Substrate portability}\label{sec:substrate-portability}

These mechanisms are independent of the underlying PIFO implementation.
Our prototype uses a shift-register-based PIFO similar to the original design, but Rio only assumes the standard push-in-first-out interface.
Other PIFO implementations can be substituted.

\subsection{Executing \(\delta\) as a transaction}\label{sec:hw-transaction}

Rio implements a transactional update controller that executes each lowered \(\delta\) as an atomic hardware transaction.
In hardware, the controller executes commands in order.
Ordinary update commands are routed to the target PEs and staged locally.
When the controller reaches the commit instruction, it generates the hardware commit signals that make the staged updates visible.
These signals are delay-aligned across PEs according to the PIFO tree topology, so that no enqueue or dequeue operation can traverse a partially updated tree.
Intuitively, the controller treats the commit boundary as a wavefront: the new configuration becomes visible at different PEs at carefully chosen times, ensuring that any individual packet observes a consistent configuration along its entire path.

The controller also implements guarded transitions.
As described in \cref{sec:sequences}, some transitions are guarded by predicates such as the emptiness of a subtree.
Rio lowers such guards to blocking commands in the controller queue.
A blocking command remains at the head of the queue until its predicate becomes true; only then can the subsequent update instructions execute.
FIFO execution therefore ensures that instructions depending on the guard are not issued prematurely.

Our current implementation uses a single controller queue, which provides a simple global serialization point for updates.
This design is sufficient for correctness and avoids subtle ordering bugs between overlapping commits.
It can, however, introduce head-of-line blocking when a guarded transition waits for a long-running drain.
Supporting multiple independent queues for disjoint subtrees is a natural optimization and does not change Rio's ISA or correctness argument; we leave this extension to future work.
